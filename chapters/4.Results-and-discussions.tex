\section{RDT mesh interface overlap: discovery and impact assessment}
\label{sec:rdt-mesh-overlap}
This chapter reports the results in two layers. The screening matrix of 18 guide-vane configurations on the original RDT mesh forms the primary layer. A smaller second layer of eight corrected-mesh reruns, the NoGV reference plus seven guide-vane anchors, was added after a setup error in the original was found during post-processing. The error and how it was found are documented in this section.

The 18-case matrix remains the primary screening result because the relevant comparison is between configurations, not against an absolute reference. The same interface defect is present in all 18 original screening simulations, so the matrix can still be interpreted comparatively. However, the corrected anchor cases show that the bias is case-dependent. Family-level trends are therefore retained as primary findings, while same-family rankings at similar chord are treated as screening-level results unless they are anchor-confirmed. No experimental dataset for this geometry exists to validate against, which reinforces the comparative framing.


\subsection{Nature of the setup error}
\label{sec:rdt-mesh-overlap-nature}

The rotor domain and the hub-pipe domain of the RDT share a cylindrical interface at $r_\mathrm{hub} = 0.120$~m. The TurboGrid mesh on the rotor blades extends $0.0326$~mm radially beyond this interface, and CFX treated the overlapping nodes as a no-slip wall during preprocessing. This creates an artificial interior wall along the hub-blade interface that blocks the recirculation through the hub-pipe interior. The effect is local to the hub and does not touch the GV passage or the cone-bend section directly.

Figure~\ref{fig:rdt-mesh-overlap-cc500} contrasts the original and corrected setup for \texttt{CC\_500\_8}. The original mesh shows discontinuous pressure bands at the hub boundary and velocity vectors that stop at the artificial wall. The corrected mesh restores continuous pressure and through-flow as expected for a hubless thruster. The swirl-only reference shows the same difference (Figure~\ref{fig:rdt-mesh-overlap-swirl}), with a lower downstream pressure level and a smoother radial gradient at the hub.

\subsection{Undetected by routine checks}
\label{sec:rdt-mesh-overlap-why-missed}

Several routine checks passed without flagging the overlap. The global mass imbalance was small, RMS residuals converged below \(10^{-5}\), and monitored integral quantities stabilised within the iteration window. These checks did not expose the local interface blockage because the displaced fluid was rerouted around the hub wall rather than lost from the domain.

The error surfaced during post-processing of velocity vectors on a $zy$-plane through the RDT. A row of stagnant vectors along the hub-blade boundary, together with a near-stagnant region inside the hub pipe, pointed to the wall. Inspection of the TurboGrid blade mesh and the hub-pipe mesh confirmed the $0.0326$~mm overlap. The RDT mesh came from parallel work in the Store2Hydro project and was combined with the meshes built for this thesis. The interface between the two was not checked closely enough before use.


\subsection{Corrected sub-sample: seven anchor cases}
\label{sec:rdt-mesh-overlap-validation}

A full rerun of all 18 configurations was not feasible in the time available. The NoGV swirl reference and seven guide-vane anchors were rerun with the corrected RDT mesh: three \texttt{CC} cases (\texttt{CC\_250\_9}, \texttt{CC\_450\_6}, \texttt{CC\_500\_8}) and four \texttt{CS} cases (\texttt{CS\_250\_9}, \texttt{CS\_450\_6}, \texttt{CS\_500\_6}, \texttt{CS\_500\_8}). The set spans both families, three chord levels, and the corners of the matrix that the Pareto analysis later identifies (Section~\ref{sec:pareto-front}).

Table~\ref{tab:correctRDT-anchor} lists the system head increase $\Delta H_\mathrm{sys} = H_\mathrm{ConeEnd} - H_\mathrm{RDTStart}$ and the mass-averaged absolute flow angle $|\alpha|$ at \texttt{ConeEnd}, before and after correction.

The NoGV reference and the GV cases move in opposite directions. Without vanes, removing the artificial hub wall lets a stronger swirl leave the RDT, so both $\Delta H_\mathrm{sys}$ and $|\alpha|$ rise ($+6.2\%$ and $+18.8\%$). With vanes, the cleaner inflow lets them recover more swirl: $\Delta H_\mathrm{sys}$ drops by $7$ to $14\%$ and $|\alpha|$ by $2$ to $32\%$. The size of the shift varies from case to case, so no single factor corrects it. The direction is the same for these two metrics, but not for every quantity. The vane-passage head loss falls for five anchors and rises for \texttt{CS\_450\_6} and \texttt{CS\_500\_6}. This sets the limit on how far the correction can be generalised.

\subsection{What survives the correction}
\label{sec:rdt-mesh-overlap-impact}

The seven anchors are too few to revalidate every ranking in the 18-case matrix, but they are enough to separate the claims that survive the correction from those that do not.

In both setups, \texttt{CC} keeps the lower vane-passage head loss and \texttt{CS} the lower residual angle. Within each family, longer chord gives lower residual angle. The head-loss versus swirl-recovery trade-off keeps its direction, with stronger recovery costing more head. What does not survive is the ranking between two cases of the same family at similar chord. The non-uniform vane-passage bias from Section~\ref{sec:rdt-mesh-overlap-validation} can flip those, so where such a ranking matters the corrected anchor value is used and the limitation is named.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Family-level and within-family chord trends are stated as primary findings.
    \item Rankings between two cases of the same family and similar chord are stated only where they hold across both setups or are anchor-confirmed. Otherwise the limitation is named.
    \item Absolute $\Delta H_\mathrm{sys}$, head-loss components and pressure recovery are comparison values, not design predictions. The $\Delta H_\mathrm{sys}$ bias is $-7$ to $-14\%$ for the GV cases (Table~\ref{tab:correctRDT-anchor}).
    \item Cavitation margins, axial forces and runner torque are qualitative diagnostics only, since the CFX reference pressure was not set for quantitative cavitation prediction.
\end{enumerate}

The 18 uncorrected cases remain the screening matrix, with corrected anchors reported alongside where the comparison is informative. A full corrected rerun of all 18 is the principal item of future work (Chapter~\ref{chap:future_work}).

\section{Reference behaviour and validation}
\label{sec:reference-and-validation}

The screening matrix produces eighteen numbers per metric. Before interpreting them, two reference simulations anchor the meaning of those numbers. The NoGV swirl reference (RDT operating without guide vanes) defines the untreated-RDT baseline for residual swirl, flow angle and bend head loss. The NoGV straight validation (no RDT, axial inlet) tests whether the cone-bend domain itself introduces artificial swirl that could contaminate the comparison. Mass conservation and the wall-resolution scope are then documented for the full \(18\)-case matrix.

\subsection{NoGV swirl reference}
\label{sec:nogv-swirl-reference}

The NoGV swirl reference uses the corrected RDT with the full downstream domain but no guide vanes, so the rotor swirl is carried through the duct unrecovered. It sets the baseline every configuration is measured against.

Table~\ref{tab:nogv-swirl-reference} lists the principal metrics in both setups. The corrected reference reaches \( |\alpha|_{\mathrm{ConeEnd}} = 63.7^\circ \), with \( |S|=1.75 \) at \texttt{BendStart} and \( |S|=1.11 \) at \texttt{ConeEnd}. The best configuration, \texttt{CS\_500\_8}, brings $|\alpha|_{\mathrm{ConeEnd}}$ down to $7.9^\circ$ corrected, an eight-fold reduction in residual angle. The bend head loss of $0.37$~m collapses to below $0.06$~m once any vane is present, an order of magnitude lower; the mechanism is treated in Section~\ref{sec:bend-hl-collapse}.

\subsection{NoGV straight validation}
\label{sec:nogv-straight-validation}

The straight-flow validation replaces the RDT with a uniform axial inlet at $U_b = 2.32$~m/s. There is no rotor, no swirl source, and no rotor-stator interface; the cone-bend domain is otherwise identical to the screening cases. It answers one question: does the cone-bend geometry itself generate in-plane velocity that could contaminate the swirl metrics?

The mean flow angle at \texttt{ConeEnd} is $6.8^\circ$, not zero. The residual comes from the $90^\circ$ bend, which drives a counter-rotating Dean-vortex pair that registers as a small mean angle on the sampling plane. This is a bend artefact, not a setup error; the mechanism is treated in Section~\ref{sec:dean-vortex}. Its scale is an order of magnitude below the rotor swirl: area-averaged swirling strength at \texttt{ConeEnd} is $2.40$~s$^{-1}$ here against $25.1$~s$^{-1}$ in the NoGV swirl reference. The domain does not produce swirl of the same order as the RDT, so the validation passes.

The $6.8^\circ$ residual sets a floor on what the screening can resolve at \texttt{ConeEnd}. Only \texttt{CS\_500\_8} ($9.1^\circ$ uncorrected, $7.9^\circ$ corrected) approaches it, so differences in the single-digit range should not be read as physical superiority. Section~\ref{sec:swirl-recovery} treats the angle scale near and below $10^\circ$ accordingly.

\section{Head increase and head-loss decomposition}
\label{sec:head-increase-and-hl}

The screening question on the pressure side is straightforward: how much head does each guide-vane configuration deliver to the downstream pump-turbine, and where in the duct is the head loss generated? The first quantity is the system head increase, $\Delta H_{\mathrm{sys}} = H_{\mathrm{ConeEnd}} - H_{\mathrm{RDTStart}}$. The second is the decomposition of the total head loss into the guide-vane passage and the downstream draft-tube section, with the \(90^\circ\) bend reported as a diagnostic sub-interval. Together they identify both the magnitude of the trade-off between configurations and the part of the duct where each configuration pays its penalty.

\subsection{System head increase across the screening matrix}
\label{sec:hi-across-matrix}

Figure~\ref{fig:hi-bar} shows $\Delta H_{\mathrm{sys}}$ for the $18$-case screening matrix, sorted from highest to lowest. The dashed horizontal line marks the corrected NoGV swirl reference at $4.21$~m.

\centering
\fbox{\includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{Figures/Results/fig_4_3_HI_bar.png}}
\caption[System head increase]{System head increase $\Delta H_{\mathrm{sys}}$ for the $18$-case matrix. Dashed line: corrected NoGV swirl reference ($4.21$~m).}
\label{fig:hi-bar}
\end{figure}

The GV bars represent the screening matrix, while the dashed reference line uses the corrected NoGV value from Table~\ref{tab:nogv-swirl-reference}. The corrected NoGV reference is used because it represents the current physical baseline without guide vanes.

The highest $\Delta H_{\mathrm{sys}}$ in the GV matrix is \texttt{CC\_400\_8} at $3.75$~m, and the lowest is \texttt{CS\_500\_8} at $3.23$~m. The range across the matrix is $0.52$~m, about $16\%$ of the median.

The NoGV reference delivers higher $\Delta H_{\mathrm{sys}}$ than any GV configuration. The reference has no GV passage and therefore no associated head loss. Any guide vane inserted in the duct trades some head for swirl reduction. The screening question is the shape of that trade-off, not whether the trade-off exists.

A simple chord rule does not predict the ordering within each family. In the CC family, \texttt{CC\_400\_8} sits at the top and \texttt{CC\_450\_8} near the bottom; the highest-HI CC configuration is not the longest-chord case. In the CS family, the longest chord corresponds to the lowest HI (\texttt{CS\_500\_8}). The two families respond differently to chord length, which Section~\ref{sec:family-and-chord-trends} examines.

The family separation is sharp across the matrix. The five highest-HI configurations all belong to the CC family. The five lowest-HI configurations all belong to the CS family. The clean split motivates a family-level statistical comparison rather than case-by-case discussion.

\subsection{Head-loss decomposition}
\label{sec:hl-decomposition}

The head-loss budget is evaluated from \texttt{GVStart} to \texttt{ConeEnd}. The guide-vane passage loss \(\mathrm{HL_{GV}}\) is taken between \texttt{GVStart} and the fixed reference plane \texttt{GVEnd\_z680}. This plane is located at \(z_{\mathrm{loc}}=0.495~\mathrm{m}\) in Table~\ref{tab:planes}, corresponding to \(z_{\mathrm{global}}\approx-0.680~\mathrm{m}\) in the CFX coordinate system, downstream of the longest vane trailing edge.

The downstream loss \(\mathrm{HL_{cone}}\) is taken between \texttt{GVEnd\_z680} and \texttt{ConeEnd}. The bend loss \(\mathrm{HL_{bend}}\) is taken between \texttt{BendStart} and \texttt{BendEnd}. It is reported separately because it isolates the loss generated in the \(90^\circ\) bend. Since the bend interval lies inside the downstream interval, \(\mathrm{HL_{bend}}\) is a diagnostic sub-interval of \(\mathrm{HL_{cone}}\), not an additional contribution to be added on top of it.

Figure~\ref{fig:hl-stacked} shows the additive head-loss budget as two stacked components: the GV-passage loss and the downstream draft-tube loss from \texttt{GVEnd\_z680} to \texttt{ConeEnd}. Figure~\ref{fig:hl-streamwise} shows the same losses accumulating along the duct. Numerical values, including the isolated bend loss, are listed in Table~\ref{tab:hl-decomposition}.

The guide-vane passage dominates the loss budget for every GV configuration, accounting for $58\%$ to $87\%$ of the total. The downstream draft-tube loss is the second largest contributor and varies most strongly with design: from \(0.146~\mathrm{m}\) for \texttt{CC\_250\_9} to \(0.779~\mathrm{m}\) for \texttt{CS\_500\_6}, a factor of more than five across the matrix. The bend loss is essentially negligible for any GV configuration, ranging from $0.014$ to $0.058$~m. The same bend section produces $0.393$~m of loss for the NoGV reference, an order of magnitude higher.

The bend collapse is the most pronounced single effect in the loss decomposition. As soon as any guide vane reduces the upstream swirl, the bend section essentially stops generating measurable head loss. The mechanism is examined in Section~\ref{sec:bend-hl-collapse}. The relevant observation for the screening interpretation is that the bend section is not where configurations differentiate. The differentiation happens in the trade-off between \(\mathrm{HL_{GV}}\) and the downstream draft-tube loss \(\mathrm{HL_{cone}}\), which the family-level analysis examines next.

The CC family delivers higher mean $\Delta H_{\mathrm{sys}}$ ($3.70$~m) than CS ($3.49$~m), a difference of $0.21$~m or about $6\%$. The CC family also shows lower mean total head loss ($1.36$~m versus $1.54$~m). The CC advantage is not the result of a single sub-section: CC loses less in the guide-vane passage ($1.07$~m mean versus $1.14$~m) and less in the cone section ($0.30$~m versus $0.41$~m). Both contributions are separately measurable in the decomposition and they reinforce each other.

The cone-section difference is the larger of the two and explains most of the family-level HL gap ($0.11$~m of the $0.18$~m total). CS configurations leave more residual swirl after the trailing edge. The constant-solidity construction concentrates more turning capability at the outer span, where the dynamic head is highest, and the strong outer-span turning produces stronger tangential gradients at \texttt{GVEnd}. These gradients then mix through the contraction and appear in $\mathrm{HL_{cone}}$. CC achieves a comparable axial-flow rotation with less aggressive outer-span turning and pays a smaller cone-mixing penalty.

A caveat applies to the angle metric. The family-mean $|\alpha|_{\mathrm{ConeEnd}}$ is similar for the two families ($23.5^\circ$ CC versus $23.0^\circ$ CS, a difference of only $0.5^\circ$). The family-mean angle is a poor summary statistic for the screening matrix because it hides a strong interaction between family and chord length. Section~\ref{sec:swirl-recovery} examines this interaction directly. The short version is that CS responds more strongly to chord: the $|\alpha|_{\mathrm{ConeEnd}}$ spread across the CS family is $26^\circ$ from \texttt{CS\_250\_9} to \texttt{CS\_500\_8}, compared to $19^\circ$ across the CC family. At the longest chord, CS clearly outperforms CC on residual angle; at the shortest chord the two families are nearly identical.

The chord trends within each family in terms of head balance are not symmetric. Within the CC family, lengthening the chord from $250$ to $500$~mm decreases $\mathrm{HL_{GV}}$ from $1.19$ to $0.81$~m and increases $\mathrm{HL_{cone}}$ from $0.15$ to $0.56$~m. The two effects nearly cancel: total head loss changes from $1.33$ to $1.37$~m and $\Delta H_{\mathrm{sys}}$ changes from $3.70$ to $3.74$~m. Lengthening the chord shifts the loss from the GV passage to the cone section, but the head balance is robust. Within the CS family the same chord change keeps $\mathrm{HL_{GV}}$ essentially flat ($1.21$ to $1.21$~m), strongly increases $\mathrm{HL_{cone}}$ from $0.20$ to $0.70$~m, and consequently lowers $\Delta H_{\mathrm{sys}}$ from $3.62$ to $3.23$~m. Lengthening the chord in the CS family adds direct cost without a compensating reduction in GV-passage loss.

The asymmetry tells the design story for $\Delta H_{\mathrm{sys}}$: the CC family is more forgiving of chord variation in head-balance terms, while the CS family pays directly for stronger swirl recovery as the chord lengthens. The swirl-recovery side of the trade-off is examined in the next section, and the combined picture appears in the Pareto analysis in Section~\ref{sec:pareto-front}.

\section{Swirl recovery and residual flow angle}
\label{sec:swirl-recovery}

Section~\ref{sec:head-increase-and-hl} examined the head side of the trade-off. The other side is the swirl reduction each configuration achieves. Two metrics carry the analysis. The mass-averaged absolute flow angle $|\alpha|$ at \texttt{ConeEnd} is the principal RQ1 metric, since \texttt{ConeEnd} is the RPT inlet plane. The swirl number $S$, defined as the axial flux of angular momentum normalised by the axial momentum flux and a reference radius, characterises how the angular-momentum content evolves through the duct. Both metrics are reported. $|\alpha|$ is dimensionally familiar and reports the local flow direction; $S$ weights tangential velocity by radius and captures where the swirl energy is located.

\subsection{Mean flow angle at \texttt{ConeEnd}}
\label{sec:mean-flow-angle}

Figure~\ref{fig:alpha-bar} shows $|\alpha|_{\mathrm{ConeEnd}}$ grouped by chord and vane count, and the swirl-number magnitudes through the duct are listed in Table~\ref{tab:swirl-wide}.

The screening matrix spans $|\alpha|_{\mathrm{ConeEnd}}$ from $9.1^\circ$ (\texttt{CS\_500\_8}) to $35.1^\circ$ (\texttt{CS\_250\_9}). The factor of nearly four between best and worst is the largest relative spread of any screening metric. Both extremes are in the same family. The CS construction is the most powerful when chord is long and the least effective when chord is short.

Long chord wins in both families. The three best configurations are \texttt{CS\_500\_8} ($9.1^\circ$), \texttt{CS\_500\_6} ($13.1^\circ$), and \texttt{CC\_500\_8} ($16.5^\circ$). The worst pair pairs near-identical angles from short-chord, high-vane-count cases: \texttt{CS\_250\_9} at $35.1^\circ$ and \texttt{CC\_250\_9} at $35.0^\circ$. The chord effect is not symmetric across families. Going from $250$ to $500$~mm drops the residual angle by $26^\circ$ in CS but only $19^\circ$ in CC. The CS family construction concentrates more turning at the outer span, where the dynamic head is highest. When the chord is long enough, that outer-span turning removes more angular momentum than the equivalent CC design and the family pulls ahead. When the chord is short, the CS family loses this advantage and the two families converge.

The four corners of the design space organise the picture cleanly. Long chord with CS gives the lowest angle. Long chord with CC gives moderate angle and the highest $\Delta H_{\mathrm{sys}}$. Short chord with CS gives the worst angle. Short chord with CC gives near-equal angle to its CS counterpart at less head cost. Intermediate cases fall along smooth interpolations between these corners.

The mesh-bias caveat from Section~\ref{sec:rdt-mesh-overlap-impact} applies. Within-family rankings between adjacent chord steps (\texttt{CC\_450\_6} versus \texttt{CC\_400\_8}, for example) are not anchor-confirmed. Family-level and corner-of-matrix comparisons are robust across both setups.

\subsection{Swirl number evolution through the duct}
\label{sec:swirl-number-evolution}

The angle metric reports a local quantity at one plane. The swirl number tracks angular-momentum flux through the duct and reveals how each configuration redistributes the tangential momentum left over from the guide vane.

At \texttt{GVStart}, the donut-region swirl number ranges from $0.82$ (\texttt{CC\_250\_9}) to $1.24$ (\texttt{CS\_500\_6}) across the GV matrix. The NoGV reference shows $S_{\mathrm{GVStart}} = 1.67$, well above any GV case. The presence of the vanes alters the upstream swirl even at the leading-edge plane through the elliptic upstream influence of GV blockage on the RDT wake. The effect is small in absolute terms but consistent: longer-chord and lower-vane-count configurations push their influence slightly further upstream.

At the deepest GVEnd plane (\texttt{GVEnd6}), $S$ collapses by a factor of two to eight relative to \texttt{GVStart}. \texttt{CS\_500\_8} drops from $1.22$ to $0.15$, an eight-fold reduction. \texttt{CC\_250\_9} drops only from $0.82$ to $0.88$, essentially unchanged, because the short chord and high vane count produce a vane stack that under-turns and leaves the donut-region swirl close to its incoming value. The contrast between these two extremes is the screening result in one comparison.

Between \texttt{GVEnd6} and \texttt{ConeEnd} the flow transitions from the annular GV region to the full pipe cross-section and undergoes contraction. The swirl number computed on the two planes is not directly comparable in magnitude, since the reference radius and area change. The magnitude collapse from \texttt{GVStart} to \texttt{GVEnd6} is the swirl-recovery result; the streamwise traces in Figures~\ref{fig:s-streamwise-cc} and~\ref{fig:s-streamwise-cs} show how it develops through the cone and bend.

The bend amplifies the swirl it receives, so weak-recovery configurations are penalised far more than strong-recovery ones: the absolute swirl number at \texttt{BendEnd} roughly doubles for low-recovery cases such as \texttt{CC\_250\_9} and \texttt{CS\_250\_9}, while the Pareto candidates barely change. The streamwise $|S|$ traces are given in Section~\ref{sec:s-streamwise} and the loss mechanism in Section~\ref{sec:bend-hl-collapse}. Across the bend the sampling plane reorients from $z$-normal upstream to $y$-normal at \texttt{BendEnd} and \texttt{ConeEnd}, so the post-bend swirl-number sign is referenced to a different axis and is not directly comparable with the upstream sign; the rise in $|S|$ through the bend is this amplification, not a breakdown of the metric.

\section{Swirl-loss trade-off: Pareto analysis}
\label{sec:pareto-front}

Sections~\ref{sec:head-increase-and-hl} and \ref{sec:swirl-recovery} examined the head side and the swirl side of the trade-off separately. The full design question is which configurations occupy the boundary between the two: the set of designs that no other design beats on both metrics simultaneously. This section identifies that boundary, the Pareto front, in the screening matrix and tests its robustness against the anchor sub-sample.

\subsection{Identification of Pareto-dominant configurations}
\label{sec:pareto-identification}
A configuration is Pareto-dominant if no other configuration in the matrix has both higher $\Delta H_{\mathrm{sys}}$ and lower $|\alpha|_{\mathrm{ConeEnd}}$. The configurations that survive this filter form the Pareto front. Designs inside the front are dominated by at least one Pareto-front member and should be selected only for reasons not captured in the two-metric trade-off.

Figure~\ref{fig:pareto-map} shows the screening matrix on the $|\alpha|_{\mathrm{ConeEnd}}$-versus-$\Delta H_{\mathrm{sys}}$ plane. The Pareto front in the uncorrected matrix contains four configurations: \texttt{CS\_500\_8}, \texttt{CS\_500\_6}, \texttt{CC\_500\_8}, and \texttt{CC\_400\_8}. All four belong to the long-chord part of the design space ($c \geq 400$~mm). The uncorrected NoGV swirl reference sits high on the plot at $|\alpha| = 53.6^\circ$ and $\Delta H_{\mathrm{sys}} = 3.96$~m and is not part of the GV Pareto front. Table~\ref{tab:final-decision} lists the four cases with their head-loss budget.

Useful swirl recovery in this geometry requires sufficient chord length to develop the turning, independent of family.

\subsection{Anchor-based robustness check}
\label{sec:pareto-anchor-check}

The Pareto front is identified from the uncorrected matrix, and the mesh correction documented in Section~\ref{sec:rdt-mesh-overlap} biases the absolute values of both metrics. The relevant question is whether the four Pareto-front cases remain Pareto-dominant after correction.

Figure~\ref{fig:pareto-combined} overlays the corrected anchors on the uncorrected matrix. Three of the four uncorrected Pareto cases are present in the anchor sub-sample: \texttt{CS\_500\_8}, \texttt{CS\_500\_6}, and \texttt{CC\_500\_8}. \texttt{CC\_400\_8} was not rerun, so the discussion that follows uses corrected data for the three anchored cases and notes the gap for \texttt{CC\_400\_8} explicitly.

All three anchor cases move consistently with their uncorrected Pareto position: both $\Delta H_{\mathrm{sys}}$ and $|\alpha|_{\mathrm{ConeEnd}}$ decrease after correction, and the angle-axis ordering of the trio is preserved, with \texttt{CS\_500\_8} lowest and \texttt{CC\_500\_8} highest. The qualitative trade-off shape is unchanged.

The head-corner versus intermediate-case comparison requires attention. In the uncorrected matrix, \texttt{CS\_500\_6} sits closer to the low-angle Pareto corner than \texttt{CC\_500\_8} ($13.1^\circ$ versus $16.5^\circ$) at lower $\Delta H_{\mathrm{sys}}$ ($3.33$~m versus $3.74$~m). Neither case dominates the other, and both are Pareto-dominant. In the corrected anchors the angle-axis order flips: \texttt{CC\_500\_8} reaches $11.3^\circ$ while \texttt{CS\_500\_6} reaches only $12.3^\circ$. Combined with the head-axis ordering ($3.33$~m for \texttt{CC\_500\_8} against $3.09$~m for \texttt{CS\_500\_6}), \texttt{CC\_500\_8} now dominates \texttt{CS\_500\_6} on both metrics. The cause is the asymmetric correction effect of Section~\ref{sec:rdt-mesh-overlap-validation}: the angle metric improves by $-31.5\%$ for \texttt{CC\_500\_8} but only $-6.3\%$ for \texttt{CS\_500\_6}. Whether \texttt{CS\_500\_6} retains Pareto status in a fully corrected $18$-case matrix cannot be settled without the remaining reruns, so the anchor data flag it as a case at risk of losing Pareto-dominance.

The untested case \texttt{CC\_400\_8} is the largest gap in the anchor validation. It differs from \texttt{CC\_500\_8} by less than $0.01$~m in $\Delta H_{\mathrm{sys}}$ but by $5^\circ$ in $|\alpha|$, so if the correction biases the two cases differently, as the other anchor pair indicates is possible, the corrected ordering may not match the uncorrected one. A corrected rerun of \texttt{CC\_400\_8} is the single highest-priority follow-up simulation from this thesis, repeated in Chapter~\ref{chap:future_work}.

The robustness assessment for the Pareto front is as follows. Two cases, \texttt{CS\_500\_8} and \texttt{CC\_500\_8}, are anchor-confirmed Pareto-dominant. One case, \texttt{CS\_500\_6}, is anchor-tested but at risk of losing Pareto status under correction. One case, \texttt{CC\_400\_8}, is untested and remains a candidate of interest pending future work. The two anchor-confirmed Pareto-dominant configurations are \texttt{CS\_500\_8} at the low-angle corner and \texttt{CC\_500\_8} at the high-head corner of the confirmed sub-sample.

\subsection{Design philosophies along the Pareto front}
\label{sec:pareto-design-philosophies}

The Pareto front in this geometry is not a single point. It is a curve along which different system priorities select different configurations.

Maximum swirl recovery selects the angle-corner case. \texttt{CS\_500\_8} delivers the lowest residual angle in the matrix and pays $0.52$~m more head loss than the head-corner case. The configuration is best when downstream RPT inlet quality dominates the design priority, when cavitation behaviour is sensitive to flow uniformity, or when residual swirl is known to interact unfavourably with components further downstream.

Maximum head delivery selects the head-corner case. \texttt{CC\_500\_8} (anchor-confirmed) and \texttt{CC\_400\_8} (uncorrected only) deliver the highest $\Delta H_{\mathrm{sys}}$ in the matrix and accept residual angles around $17$ to $22^\circ$. These configurations are appropriate when the boosting function is the dominant design driver, when the downstream RPT is robust to moderate inlet swirl, or when total system efficiency over the operating range is the primary metric.

No single configuration is the optimum in this trade-off. The Pareto front is the answer to the screening question, not a single point on it. Section~\ref{sec:synthesis} returns to the candidate selection with the additional information accumulated through the remaining sections of this chapter, including the downstream flow conditioning behaviour in the cone and bend, the spanwise diagnostics at \texttt{ConeEnd}, and the FFT analysis of the transient RDT-GV interaction.

\section{Downstream flow conditioning: cone and bend}
\label{sec:downstream-conditioning}

So far the matrix has been evaluated at \texttt{ConeEnd}, the plane that sets the RPT inlet condition. The path there from \texttt{GVEnd} runs through the contraction and the $90^\circ$ bend, and neither is passive. The cone redistributes the residual swirl as the section narrows, and the bend adds secondary flow that interacts with the swirl arriving at \texttt{BendStart}. This section explains the bend head-loss collapse from Section~\ref{sec:hl-decomposition} and follows how the residual swirl develops past the screening plane.

\subsection{Bend secondary flow and the head-loss collapse}
\label{sec:bend-hl-collapse}
\label{sec:dean-vortex}

The bend produces about $0.37$~m of head loss in the NoGV swirl reference but below $0.06$~m once any guide vane is present (Table~\ref{tab:hl-decomposition}), an order-of-magnitude reduction. The reason is how a $90^\circ$ bend responds to the swirl it receives. A near-axial inflow drives only the intrinsic counter-rotating Dean pair, a closed secondary flow that dissipates locally at little cost. A strongly swirling inflow cannot form this symmetric pair. An asymmetric structure develops instead, and the bend turns the rotational energy into mixing loss.

Figure~\ref{fig:dean-montage} and Table~\ref{tab:dean-secondary} show the \texttt{BendEnd} secondary-flow field for six reference cases and sort them into three regimes. The straight inflow develops the textbook Dean pair, with net in-plane swirl $S_{\mathrm{net}} \approx 0$ and a peak in-plane velocity below half the bulk velocity. The untreated RDT wake is swirl dominated, at $S_{\mathrm{net}} = 1.7$ and a peak above three bulk velocities. The guide vanes fall between these limits. The long-chord \texttt{CC\_500\_8} and \texttt{CS\_500\_8} cut $S_{\mathrm{net}}$ to about $0.13$ and sit close to the straight reference, while the short-chord \texttt{CC\_250\_9} and \texttt{CS\_250\_9} under-turn the wake and hold $S_{\mathrm{net}} \approx 0.5$.

\subsection{Vortex-core content from the Q-criterion}
\label{sec:q-criterion-analysis}

The swirl number and the flow angle measure bulk rotation on a plane. They do not separate strong coherent vortices from weak rotation spread across the section. The Q-criterion of Eq.~\eqref{eq:Q_def} adds that separation: \(Q>0\) marks rotation-dominated fluid, so the positive-\(Q\) field isolates the vane-wake cores and the Dean pair in the bend.

Three positive-\(Q\) metrics are used per plane: the area fraction \(f_{Q>0}\), the mean positive value \(\overline{Q}^{+}\), and the strong-core fraction \(f_{Q>Q_{\mathrm{ref}}}\) with the threshold of Eq.~\eqref{eq:Q_ref}. At \texttt{ConeEnd} the area fraction \(f_{Q>0}\) saturates between \(0.65\) and \(0.80\) for all cases, including the references, so it does not separate the designs. The strong-core fraction and the mean positive value do, and are used below.

\section{Radial flow quality at the RPT-inlet proxy}
\label{sec:radial-flow-quality}

Two screening metrics have carried the analysis so far: $|\alpha|_{\mathrm{ConeEnd}}$ and $S_{\mathrm{ConeEnd}}$. Both are bulk quantities averaged across the full cross-section. Mass-flow averaging weights each radial position by the local axial mass flux. In an annular flow the area element scales with $r$, and the axial velocity is typically larger at the outer radii. The bulk metric therefore weights the outer radii more heavily than the inner radii. A configuration with strong outer-band recovery and weak inner-band recovery can produce a low bulk $|\alpha|$ value while leaving a non-uniform radial profile. The cavitation behaviour of the downstream pump-turbine depends on the inner radii where static pressure is lowest, and the bulk metric carries little information about that band.

This section verifies that the Pareto-front configurations achieve their bulk ranking through proportionate recovery across the radial span, rather than through outer-band recovery alone. Figure~\ref{fig:radial-profiles} shows the absolute flow angle $|\alpha|(r/R)$ at \texttt{ConeEnd} for the NoGV swirl reference and four guide-vane configurations: \texttt{CS\_500\_8}, \texttt{CC\_500\_8}, \texttt{CC\_400\_8}, and \texttt{CC\_250\_9}.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{Figures/Results/fig_4_9_alpha_radial.png}
\caption[Radial flow angle at \texttt{ConeEnd}]{Radial distribution of the absolute flow angle $|\alpha|(r/R)$ at \texttt{ConeEnd}, for the NoGV reference, the Pareto candidates and two short-chord contrast cases, with the remaining configurations in grey. Velocity components are given in Appendix~\ref{app:appD}.}
\label{fig:radial-profiles}
\end{figure}

The NoGV reference shows the rim-driven wake structure carried through to \texttt{ConeEnd}. Tangential velocity peaks near the outer radii where the RDT injects most of the swirl, and the local flow angle follows the same pattern. The mass-flow weighting of the bulk metric amplifies the outer-band contribution, which is consistent with the bulk value of $63.7^\circ$ in the corrected setup. The inner radii carry lower angles but cover less mass flow and contribute less to the bulk average.

\texttt{CS\_500\_8} produces the most uniform radial profile of the configurations shown. The radial spread of $|\alpha|$ between inner and outer band is the smallest in the set, and the entire profile sits close to the bulk value of $9.1^\circ$. The constant-solidity construction places more chord at the outer radii because the circumferential spacing grows with radius, and constant solidity requires longer chord to maintain the same blade-to-blade spacing. The turning capability is therefore concentrated exactly where the RDT wake requires it. The case combines strong inner-span recovery with the lowest bulk angle in the matrix and is the most balanced candidate on the radial axis.

\texttt{CC\_500\_8} produces a less uniform profile with stronger relative reduction at the outer band than at the inner band. The fixed-chord construction distributes turning capability uniformly across the span, which matches the spanwise demand of the rim-driven wake less precisely than the CS construction does. The configuration's high $H_I$ in the anchor-confirmed set partly reflects the lower wetted area at the outer radii compared to the CS counterpart at the same nominal chord. The modestly non-uniform radial profile is the cost of that head advantage.

\texttt{CC\_400\_8} sits between the two cases above on every radial measure. The shorter chord reduces overall turning capability, and the residual profile is correspondingly less recovered than either anchor-confirmed Pareto case. The bias caveat from Section~\ref{sec:rdt-mesh-overlap-impact} applies: without an anchor rerun, the radial profile cannot be cross-checked against the corrected setup. The radial diagnostics for this case should therefore be read qualitatively against the other three.

\texttt{CC\_250\_9} retains the rim-driven wake structure at reduced amplitude. The outer band carries the dominant tangential velocity and the largest residual flow angle, while the inner band recovers more effectively because it receives less wake content to begin with. The bulk value of $35.0^\circ$ reflects under-correction concentrated in the band that dominates the mass-flow integral.

The radial diagnostics support the bulk-metric ranking established in Section~\ref{sec:swirl-recovery}. Configurations with low $|\alpha|_{\mathrm{ConeEnd}}$ produce uniform radial profiles. Configurations with high $|\alpha|_{\mathrm{ConeEnd}}$ produce profiles that retain the outer-biased wake structure. No Pareto candidate hides inner-span weakness behind a strong outer-band recovery, and the screening's bulk ranking is therefore representative of the radial-resolved flow quality at \texttt{ConeEnd}.

One caveat closes the radial analysis. The band $r/R \lesssim 0.3$ carries the lowest static pressure in the cone region and is the most cavitation-sensitive radial position for the downstream pump-turbine. The bulk $|\alpha|$ metric does not weight this band heavily because the axial mass flux is small there. A full-system assessment of inner-span cavitation behaviour requires coupled RDT-GV-RPT simulations as flagged in Section~\ref{sec:pareto-anchor-check}. The radial diagnostics in this section confirm that the screening's bulk metric is representative of the cross-section structure at \texttt{ConeEnd}, not that the inner-span profile is sufficient for the downstream pump-turbine.

\label{sec:results-fft}

The two cases are diagnostic blade-count configurations; the final hydraulic candidate is selected separately from the steady screening matrix. \texttt{CC\_450\_7} is the project-work baseline ($Z_r = Z_s = 7$, $m_{\min} = 0$); the Tyler-Sofrin analysis predicts a synchronous breathing mode at $f_{\mathrm{BPF}}$. \texttt{CC\_400\_6} is the spinning-mode comparator ($Z_s = 6$, $m_{\min} = 1$) drawn from the screening matrix. Both runs were initialised from the same baseline restart at $t = 2.447$~s and continued for 15 rotor revolutions to $t = 3.831$~s.

\paragraph{Observation 1: blade-passing forcing is weak.} At $f_{\mathrm{BPF}}$ the cyclic force on a single vane is $0.56$~N for \texttt{CC\_450\_7} and $1.71$~N for \texttt{CC\_400\_6}, which is $0.07\,\%$ and $0.27\,\%$ of the respective mean vane loads ($\approx 800$~N and $\approx 630$~N). Both lie below the $0.5\,\%$ level adopted here as a diagnostic cut-off for deterministic blade passing forcing, consistent with the RSI amplitude levels reported by Egusquiza et al.~\cite{Egusquiza2006}. The rotor-frequency content exceeds the blade-passing content in both cases(Table~\ref{tab:peak_amplitudes}), so the unsteady vane loading is not driven by deterministic blade passing.

\paragraph{Observation 2: pressure pulsations are well below the RSI threshold.}
The normalised pulsation at the RDT-GV interface is
\begin{equation}
    \tilde{p}_{E}
    = \frac{A_p(f_{\mathrm{BPF}})}{\rho g H}
    \approx 0.04\text{-}0.06\,\%,
    \label{eq:normalised-pulsation}
\end{equation}
more than one order of magnitude below the $1\,\%$ threshold of~\cite{Brekke2001,Brekke2010} for significant rotor-stator interaction.

\paragraph{Observation 3: the equal blade-count case does not show a stronger breathing-mode signature.}
\texttt{CC\_400\_6} ($m_{\min} = 1$) shows higher relative blade-passing content than \texttt{CC\_450\_7} ($m_{\min} = 0$): the ratio $A(f_{\mathrm{BPF}})/A(f_n)$ for $F_{\mathrm{res}}$ is $0.58$ in \texttt{CC\_400\_6} and $0.11$ in \texttt{CC\_450\_7}. This is not the behaviour expected from the compact-stage Tyler-Sofrin argument, where the equal blade-count case is the main concern. The absolute amplitudes remain low in both cases, and the expected coherent breathing-mode signature is weak in the present geometry. A physical interpretation is given in Section~\ref{sec:results-fft-physicalexpl}.

\subsection{Physical interpretation: large axial gap and non-uniform inflow}
\label{sec:results-fft-physicalexpl}

The absent breathing-mode signature is consistent with the large axial spacing. The gap is $a/L_{\mathrm{mid}} \approx 1.39$, about $9$ times the compact axial-pump bound (Section~\ref{sec:methods_axial_spacing}), so the potential-interaction component is strongly attenuated before the flow reaches the guide-vane row and the rotor wake has room to mix before it meets the vanes. This weakens the coherent $m = 0$ build-up predicted for a compact rotor-stator stage.

The velocity contour in Figure~\ref{fig:vel_contour_400_6} supports this interpretation. The flow is non-uniform between adjacent vane passages and shows regions of reduced velocity, consistent with recirculation in the annular space between the rim and the duct wall. The guide vanes are exposed to a mixed and asymmetric RDT wake rather than a clean $Z_r$-periodic rotor wake. A non-periodic inflow reduces the coherent build-up of the $m = 0$ component predicted by the Tyler-Sofrin framework, even when $Z_r = Z_s$.

\centering
\fbox{\includegraphics[width=0.55\linewidth]{Figures/FFT/CFD/vel_plot_CC_400_6_z048.png}}
\caption[Velocity magnitude, \texttt{CC\_400\_6}]{Velocity magnitude contour at $z = 0.48$~m for \texttt{CC\_400\_6}, just upstream of the GV trailing edge.}

This interpretation matches the dominant sub-rotor-frequency content in the monitor signals. Low-frequency fluctuations are the signature of slowly evolving recirculation, not deterministic blade-passing interaction.

The higher single-vane blade-passing content in \texttt{CC\_400\_6} fits the same mechanism. The plane-averaged inlet pressure carries a comparable $m=0$ component in both cases ($21.9$~Pa versus $28.1$~Pa), so the larger single-vane $f_{\mathrm{BPF}}$ amplitude in \texttt{CC\_400\_6} reflects local wake-vane interaction on one vane, not a stronger coherent ring mode.

\subsection{Implications for blade-count selection}
\label{sec:results-fft-implications}

The transient analysis establishes two results. The unsteady loading produced by the RDT-GV setup is below industrial concern thresholds for both configurations at the nominal operating point. No clear resonance signature is present in either case. The low coherent ring loading is interpreted as a consequence of the large axial gap and the asymmetric inflow rather than as proof that the equal blade-count configuration is generally safe.

The final guide-vane design is therefore taken from the subset with $Z_s \neq Z_r$, a conservative choice in line with the classical guideline~\cite{Brekke2001,Brekke2010,Tanaka2011,Binama2021FlowInstability} of avoiding equal rotor and stator counts, especially where acoustic or structural resonance may interact with the spatial mode order. The selection among $Z_s \in \{6, 8, 9\}$ is then made on the hydraulic metrics in the next section. Because no modal analysis of the guide vane is performed here, the fatigue-margin conclusion holds only if the lowest wet natural frequency is well separated from $f_{\mathrm{BPF}} = 75.83$~Hz; a finite-element modal analysis with added-mass effects is recommended in Chapter~\ref{chap:future_work}.

\section{Static hydraulic load on a single vane}
\label{sec:vane-load}

The steady load on one vane sets the scale for any later structural check. The hydraulic force is integrated over one vane surface in CFX, giving the resultant $F_{\mathrm{res}} = \sqrt{F_x^2 + F_y^2 + F_z^2}$ and its angle $\theta_{\mathrm{ax}}$ to the axial direction, where $\theta_{\mathrm{ax}} = 90^\circ$ is a pure cross-plane load. Both are listed for the 18 configurations in Table~\ref{tab:vane-force}, with corrected anchors below their original case.

The direction is the clear result. $\theta_{\mathrm{ax}}$ stays from about $73^\circ$ to $89^\circ$ throughout, so the vane carries a bending load rather than an axial one. Figure~\ref{fig:vane-force-cshroud} normalises $F_{\mathrm{res}}$ by the shroud chord; the intensity climbs with chord and runs higher for the CS family, matching the swirl-recovery ranking of Section~\ref{sec:swirl-recovery}: the cases that turn the flow hardest carry the most load. The corrected anchors (green) shift case by case.

$\theta_{\mathrm{ax}}$ resolves only part of the direction. It separates the axial load from the cross-plane load, but not the cross-plane part into radial and circumferential. The vane load is essentially cascade lift, acting in the tangential-axial plane with a small radial part, so the large cross-plane load is read here as the circumferential reaction to swirl removal. A cylindrical force split ($F_r$, $F_\theta$, $F_z$) on the vane surface would confirm the small radial part, and the hub-to-shroud twist that rotates the chord-normal along the span makes the bending-relevant resolution part of the structural follow-up. These are steady single-vane loads from the uncorrected RANS setup, a load scale rather than a structural result; no modal or stress analysis is done here.

\section{Synthesis and design recommendation}
\label{sec:synthesis}

The screening matrix has been characterised through five analytical axes in the preceding sections: head delivery (Section~\ref{sec:head-increase-and-hl}), swirl recovery (Section~\ref{sec:swirl-recovery}), the Pareto trade-off (Section~\ref{sec:pareto-front}), downstream flow conditioning (Section~\ref{sec:downstream-conditioning}), and radial flow quality (Section~\ref{sec:radial-flow-quality}). The transient diagnostic in Section~\ref{sec:results-fft} adds a blade-count-selection dimension. This section integrates the five axes into a multi-criteria shortlist, states a design recommendation, separates the original contribution from the inherited Store2Hydro foundation, and closes with limitations.

\subsection{Recommended candidates}
\label{sec:recommended-candidates}

The recommendation depends on which downstream priority dominates the system design. The configurations recommended for coupled RDT-GV-RPT simulations are stated below, together with the principal follow-up case.

The primary flow-conditioning candidate is \texttt{CS\_500\_8}. The configuration achieves the lowest bulk $|\alpha|_{\mathrm{ConeEnd}}$ in the matrix, produces the most uniform radial profile, and is anchor-confirmed. The case is appropriate when downstream RPT inlet quality dominates the design priority, when cavitation behaviour is sensitive to flow uniformity, or when residual swirl is known to interact unfavourably with downstream components.

For head preservation, the primary candidate is \texttt{CC\_500\_8}. The configuration delivers the highest anchor-confirmed $\Delta H_{\mathrm{sys}}$ and accepts a moderate residual angle in exchange. The case is appropriate when the boosting function is the dominant design driver, when the downstream RPT is robust to moderate inlet swirl, or when total system efficiency over the operating range is the primary metric.

\texttt{CC\_400\_8} is the principal follow-up case. The uncorrected screening places this configuration at the head corner of the Pareto front, combining high $\Delta H_{\mathrm{sys}}$ with moderate $|\alpha|$. The configuration is not anchor-confirmed, and the open question on its corrected-mesh Pareto status (Section~\ref{sec:pareto-anchor-check}) carries direct design implications.

A blade-count constraint applies to the final selection regardless of which configuration is taken forward. The FFT analysis in Section~\ref{sec:results-fft} demonstrated that the two diagnostic transient cases both produce hydraulic pulsation amplitudes well below the $1\%$ threshold of Brekke~\cite{Brekke2010,Brekke2001}. The screening result for blade-count selection is therefore not driven by FFT amplitudes but by the Tyler-Sofrin rotor-stator-interaction theory: $Z_s = 7$ produces a synchronous breathing mode that excites the whole vane stack in phase, which is undesirable from a structural and fatigue perspective even when hydraulic amplitudes are low. The recommendation is therefore to avoid $Z_s = Z_r$ in the final selection. \texttt{CS\_500\_8}, \texttt{CC\_500\_8}, and \texttt{CC\_400\_8} all use $Z_s = 8$, and the shortlist is consistent with the blade-count guidance.

\subsection{Contribution relative to previous Store2Hydro work}
\label{sec:contribution}

The present thesis builds on the Store2Hydro project at the NTNU Waterpower Laboratory. The RDT design that supplies the upstream wake follows the published methodology of Djupesland and Trivedi~\cite{DjupeslandTrivedi2025}, which combines 1D meanline analysis, 2D XFoil-based hydrofoil simulations, and Gaussian Process Regression surrogate models.

The original contribution of the present thesis is the guide-vane stage downstream of the RDT, concentrated in five elements:

\begin{enumerate}
\item Systematic 18-case CFD screening across the constant-chord and constant-solidity families in the integrated RDT-GV-draft-tube domain.
\item Discovery of the RDT hub-blade mesh-interface overlap and design of the seven-anchor corrected-mesh validation layer that isolates which screening conclusions are robust against the bias (Section~\ref{sec:rdt-mesh-overlap-impact}).
\item Construction of the multi-plane post-processing framework, including the donut-region and full-pipe swirl numbers, the streamwise plane stack from \texttt{GVStart} to \texttt{BendEnd}, and in-plane secondary-flow diagnostics at \texttt{ConeEnd}.
\item Characterisation of the bend secondary-flow regimes and the binary head-loss collapse, separating the straight Dean pair, the swirl-dominated untreated wake, and the weak-residual guide-vane cases (Section~\ref{sec:dean-vortex}).
\item Anchor-confirmed shortlist of \texttt{CS\_500\_8} and \texttt{CC\_500\_8} as primary candidates for coupled RDT-GV-RPT simulations, with \texttt{CC\_400\_8} as the highest-priority follow-up rerun.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Limitations and future work}
\label{sec:limitations}

Several limitations bound the recommendation above and motivate the follow-up work outlined in Chapter~\ref{chap:future_work}.

The screening covers one operating point and one inlet wake. The configurations were evaluated at the design mass flow and rotational speed only. Off-design behaviour, in particular reduced-flow operation where the RDT wake structure shifts, is not addressed. The Pareto ranking is therefore conditional on operation near the design point.

Cavitation behaviour is not modelled. The screening reports static pressure and residual swirl at the cross-section level but performs no cavitation inception or breakdown calculation. The inner-band cavitation sensitivity flagged in Section~\ref{sec:radial-flow-quality} is left unquantified. Full assessment requires coupled RDT-GV-RPT cavitation simulations.

Beyond the hydraulic scope, structural and fatigue behaviour is not assessed. The FFT analysis quantifies hydraulic pulsation amplitudes only. A wet-modal analysis of the candidate GV geometries, accounting for added-mass effects of the surrounding water, is required before any final design decision. The recommendation in Section~\ref{sec:recommended-candidates} is hydraulic only.

Seven anchor cases cover the corrected-mesh validation, not the full eighteen. The Pareto-front status of \texttt{CS\_500\_6} and \texttt{CC\_400\_8} under correction is partially open, as discussed in Section~\ref{sec:pareto-anchor-check}. Reruns of these two cases would close the most consequential gap in the screening's robustness.

Section~\ref{sec:dean-vortex} shows that the bend interaction is not strictly additive but does not decompose the secondary flow into rotational components. Post-processing the secondary-flow field on the corrected-mesh anchor cases would quantify the bend response per case.

Inherited modelling assumptions include the choice of steady RANS with the SST turbulence model, the geometric simplifications of the RDT hub and rim, and the imposed inlet flow conditions taken from the upstream domain. These assumptions are documented in Chapter~\ref{chap:theory_method} and apply uniformly across all \(18\) configurations. The corrected anchor cases show, however, that some same-family rankings can be case-sensitive. Family-level trends and large design separations are therefore retained as primary screening findings, while close rankings remain screening-level results unless they are anchor-confirmed.
