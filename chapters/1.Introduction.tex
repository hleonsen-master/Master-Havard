\section{Background and motivation}
\label{sec:background_motivation}

The European electricity system is integrating a growing share of variable renewable generation. The challenge has shifted from installed capacity to balancing supply and demand on short timescales~\cite{IEA_Renewables2023, EC_EGD_2021}. Hydropower with reservoirs and pumped-storage hydropower (PSH) are the only options that can provide this flexibility at continental scale and at acceptable cost~\cite{IRENA_Storage2017}.

Globally, 189~GW of pumped-storage capacity is installed, with a pipeline of new projects close to 600~GW~\cite{IHA_WHO_2025}. In Norway, hydropower covers about 90\,\% of national production~\cite{RegjeringenVannkrafthistorie2019} and Norwegian reservoirs hold roughly half of all European hydropower storage~\cite{NVE_Fleksibilitet2023}. This motivates the interest in upgrading existing Norwegian high-head plants for pumped-storage operation~\cite{DagsvikStorli2021}.

A cost-effective route is to retrofit the powerhouse with a reversible pump-turbine (RPT) instead of the original Francis runner~\cite{DagsvikStorli2021}. This reuses the existing waterways and reservoirs. The RPT operates as a Francis turbine in one direction and as a pump when rotation is reversed~\cite{Olimstad2012}. The dual-mode requirement is the main design conflict for the runner. A runner that performs well in turbine mode usually performs worse in pump mode, and the cavitation limits in pump mode are stricter. Dagsvik's work shows that the available submergence in typical Norwegian high-head plants is often insufficient for an adequate net positive suction head (NPSH) margin in pump mode~\cite{DagsvikThesis2024}.

Cavitation is therefore the main hydraulic limitation for RPT retrofits at high-head sites~\cite{Franc2007}. IEC~60193 requires a clear NPSH margin between the available head and the head at which a 1\,\% efficiency drop occurs~\cite{IEC60193_2019}. Recent measurements on the NTNU RPT confirm that the cavitation margin is a limiting design parameter~\cite{Kerlefsen2025}. Two solutions exist. The first is to lower the runner by excavating the powerhouse; the civil cost is usually prohibitive in existing plants. The second is a booster pump in the draft tube upstream of the RPT~\cite{Valstad2018}. The booster raises the local pressure in pump mode without civil works. Earlier Store2Hydro work indicates that a booster of moderate head ($H \approx 5$~m) can provide the pressure increase required to improve the cavitation margin~\cite{Hage2020}.

The booster has tight constraints. It must fit inside a draft tube of about $0.5$~m diameter, allow free passage when not in use, and not block reverse rotation in turbine mode. A conventional shaft-driven axial booster cannot meet these requirements because the shaft and hub block the centre of the duct. The hubless rim-driven thruster (RDT) places the electric motor in the duct rim and drives the blades through a rotating ring~\cite{Cao2012}. The hubless geometry leaves the centre of the duct open, which fits a draft-tube installation. Within Store2Hydro, the RDT has been adopted as the booster concept, and a sequence of project and master theses has developed its blade-element and 2D optimisation methodologies~\cite{Fjuk2025, Ambjornsson2025}.

The RDT introduces a new problem. Without a stator, the rotor leaves a swirling and radially non-uniform wake at its outlet. Marine RDT studies show that the wake is dominated by helical tip and secondary vortices that persist many duct diameters downstream~\cite{Gaggero2023}. Without a downstream stator, a substantial part of the developed head is left in the flow as residual swirl~\cite{Andersen2014}. Axial pre-rotation at the RPT inlet affects both head and cavitation in pump mode. Positive pre-rotation reduces head and aggravates cavitation. Negative pre-rotation preserves head better but still reduces efficiency relative to swirl-free inflow~\cite{Jenssen2020, Larsen2019}. The standard solution is a row of stationary swirl-recovery guide vanes (GVs) downstream of the RDT. The GVs reduce residual swirl and convert tangential kinetic energy into static pressure before the flow reaches the RPT runner.

\section{Project work and the gap addressed by this thesis}
\label{sec:project_work_gap}

The present thesis is a direct continuation of the project work \emph{Parametric Hydraulic Design of Guide Vanes for a Rim-Driven Thruster in Pumped-Storage Hydropower}~\cite{Leonsen2025}. The project work produced three results: a parametric MATLAB tool, \texttt{vanedesign\_loft\_export}, that generates NACA-type GV sections and exports 3D geometries; a CFD-based slip database condensed into a response-surface model; and a first comparative CFD study of two GV geometries (Case~A and Case~B) in a short straight duct, which showed the trade-off between swirl reduction and total-pressure loss.

Three gaps remain. First, only two 3D geometries were assessed with CFD. Two data points indicate a trade-off, but they do not map the design space. The project work explicitly lists this limited screening as a gap to close in further work. The project-work CFD domain was a straight pipe. It did not include the conical contraction and the $90^\circ$ bend which is to be introduced in a new Draft tube to be implemented in the Test Rig at Waterpower Laboratory at NTNU. The parallel RPT pump-mode simulations by Dokset~\cite{Dokset2025} used inlet profiles extracted \emph{downstream} of the GVs, so the reported RPT performance numbers exclude the GV passage drag. The design with the best RPT inflow may therefore not be the design with the best full-system behaviour.

The present thesis addresses the first two gaps directly. The slip-correction methodology from the project work is reused with a fixed value $\delta_{\mathrm{TE}} = 6^\circ$, following G\"ulich~\cite{Gulich2010}. The screening then simulates 18 GV configurations in the full RDT-GV-draft-tube domain. The matrix combines two chord-control strategies (constant chord and constant solidity at the shroud), six shroud-chord levels and three vane counts. A nineteenth simulation, denoted \texttt{NoGV}, replaces the GV region with an empty pipe and provides the reference for the swirl-reduction and head-loss metrics. Coupling with the RPT is left as future work (Chapter~\ref{chap:future_work}); the parallel thesis by Dokset~\cite{Dokset2025} covers the RPT side.


\section{Goal and research questions} %se videre på dette 
\label{sec:goal_research_q}

The main goal of this thesis is

\medskip
\begin{quote}
\textit{To develop a systematic CFD screening methodology for swirl-recovery guide vanes downstream of the rim-driven thruster in the Store2Hydro booster-RPT concept. The methodology is then used to identify how the main design variables govern the trade-off between swirl reduction and hydraulic loss, and how the residual swirl develops through the conical section, the $90^\circ$ bend, and the final inlet section before the RPT.}
\end{quote}
\medskip

This goal is broken down into four research questions:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item How do GV design parameters - vane count $Z_s$, chord-control strategy (CC vs CS), and shroud-chord magnitude - influence swirl reduction, residual swirl distribution and total-pressure loss in the integrated RDT-GV-draft-tube system?
  \item Which spanwise and plane-averaged metrics best discriminate between GV designs that condition the inflow well for the RPT and designs that do not?
  \item Is the design with the lowest residual tangential velocity directly downstream of the GVs also the best design at the bend exit, or does the bend redistribute residual swirl and change the ranking?
  \item How robust is the design ranking when evaluated across several axial planes between the GVs and the RPT inlet, and what mechanism limits the achievable swirl reduction in this geometry?
\end{enumerate}

A working hypothesis is formulated for each question. Increasing solidity, either through longer chord or higher vane count, is expected to reduce residual swirl, but at the cost of higher total-pressure loss. The best designs are therefore expected to lie on a Pareto front, not at the maximum-recovery extreme. Residual swirl is also expected to interact with the bend secondary flow, so two designs with similar swirl numbers immediately downstream of the GVs may differ at the bend exit.

\section{Scope and limitations}
\label{sec:scope_limitations}

The scope is set by previous work and by available computational resources. The following points defines it;

\paragraph{Decoupling from the RPT.} The domain starts at the RDT inlet and ends $1.0$~m past the RPT location in a straight pipe. The flow passes through the RDT, the GV section, a conical contraction from $D = 0.5$~m to $D = 0.4$~m over $1.5$~m, and a $90^\circ$ bend. The RPT is not modelled; it is replaced by the extended straight pipe, which also buffers the bend from the outlet boundary. All 18 configurations use this decoupled domain under the same upstream wake. The reasons for the decoupling and its effect on the results are set out in Section~\ref{sec:rpt-decoupling-justification}, and a coupled simulation of the selected candidate is left as future work (Chapter~\ref{chap:future_work}).

\paragraph{Steady RANS for the screening.} The 18 cases use steady RANS with the SST $k$-$\omega$ turbulence model. Two transient simulations of selected configurations cover the rotor-stator interaction analysis in Chapter~\ref{chap:results_discussion}. A full transient screening was not feasible due to computational costs.

\medskip
The following limitations from the project work are inherited:
\begin{itemize}
  \item the RDT wake used to design the GVs corresponds to a single RDT geometry and a single operating point;
  \item cavitation is not modelled in the screening campaign, as this is focused in Aleksander Doksets parallell work %SITER MASTEREN HANS;
  \item structural strength, fatigue and vibration of the GVs are not analysed beyond the unsteady-loading diagnostics in Chapter~\ref{chap:results_discussion};
  \item the slip correction is fixed at $\delta_{\mathrm{TE}} = 6^\circ$ in all configurations.
\end{itemize}

The thesis is therefore a structured screening study. It reduces a large design space to a small number of candidates and quantifies the trade-off between swirl reduction and total-pressure loss. Coupled RDT-GV-RPT simulation, cavitation assessment, and experimental validation of the shortlisted design are left for future work (Chapter~\ref{chap:future_work}).

\section{Thesis outline} %RETT: Skriv så denne stemmer til slutt
\label{sec:thesis_outline}

The thesis is organised as follows. Chapter~\ref{chap:litterature} reviews the literature on rim-driven thrusters, swirling pipe flow, GV design, slip correlations, swirl decay, and secondary flow in curved ducts. Chapter~\ref{chap:theory_method} presents the theoretical framework and the design methodology in two parts: governing equations, swirl metrics, cascade theory and rotor-stator theory in the first; the 18-case design matrix, the multi-domain CFX setup, the GCI study and the spectral methodology in the second. Chapter~\ref{chap:results_discussion} presents the simulation results and the unsteady analysis of the two transient runs. Chapter~\ref{chap:conclusion} concludes. Chapter~\ref{chap:future_work} lists recommended directions.
