\section{Background and motivation}
\label{sec:chap1_motivation}
 
The European power system is being rebuilt around variable renewable generation, and the limiting constraint is no longer how much capacity is installed but how flexibly it can be dispatched. Pumped-storage hydropower is the only mature technology that delivers this flexibility at the scale of a continental grid, and retrofitting existing high-head plants with a reversible pump-turbine (RPT) instead of the original Francis runner is the most cost-effective way of adding new pumped-storage capacity in countries with a mature hydropower portfolio~\cite{DagsvikStorli2021, IHA_WHO_2025}. In Norway, where reservoirs hold approximately half of the European hydropower storage~\cite{NVE_Fleksibilitet2023}, this argument has driven a sequence of research projects on RPT retrofits at the NTNU Waterpower Laboratory.
 
The technical limit of an RPT retrofit is cavitation in pump mode. The Francis-runner submergence available in a typical Norwegian high-head powerhouse is too small to give the RPT runner an adequate net positive suction head margin, and lowering the runner physically by excavating the powerhouse is rarely affordable~\cite{DagsvikThesis2024, Kerlefsen2025}. The Store2Hydro programme at NTNU is built around an alternative: an axial booster pump installed in the draft tube upstream of the RPT in pump mode, raising the local pressure without civil works. The booster is realised as a hubless rim-driven thruster (RDT), which leaves the centre of the duct open and so does not interfere with reverse-flow operation in turbine mode. A parallel sequence of theses in the programme has developed the basic blade-element and two-dimensional optimisation methodologies for the RDT itself.
 
The RDT, however, leaves a strongly swirling and radially non-uniform wake at its discharge. Without a downstream stator the residual swirl reaches the RPT runner as pre-rotation and reduces both head and cavitation margin in pump mode. A row of stationary swirl-recovery guide vanes (GVs) is therefore inserted between the RDT and the RPT, and the design of these vanes is the subject of the present master thesis. \section{Project work and what is left open}
\label{sec:chap1_project_gap}
 
The present thesis is a direct continuation of the project work~\cite{Leonsen2025}. The project work delivered a parametric MATLAB tool that generates NACA-type guide-vane sections from spanwise inlet data and exports them as three-dimensional geometries for CAD and CFD, a CFD-based slip database condensed into a smooth response-surface model, and a first comparative study of two example guide-vane geometries that demonstrated qualitatively the trade-off between swirl reduction and total-pressure loss.
 
Three things are left open after that work. The screening covered only two geometries, which is enough to detect a trade-off but not enough to map it. The CFD domain was a straight pipe and did not include the conical contraction, the $90^\circ$ bend or the final inlet section that the actual Store2Hydro layout places downstream of the guide vanes; the project work could therefore not say how a given guide-vane design behaves once its outflow has been processed by the bend. And the project work treated the guide-vane row as a steady aerodynamic component, even though the chosen baseline configuration with $Z_r = Z_s = 7$ blades produces, on classical Tyler--Sofrin grounds, an unsteady pressure pulsation with a spatial-mode structure that warrants direct investigation.
 
\section{Aim and research questions}
\label{sec:chap1_aim}
 
The aim of the master thesis is to close these three gaps within the available time. Concretely:
 
\begin{enumerate}
  \item to screen a structured matrix of twenty-four guide-vane configurations spanning vane count, shroud-chord magnitude and chord-control strategy, all in the same multi-domain CFD setup, and to identify the Pareto-optimal designs in the trade-off between residual swirl and total-pressure loss;
  \item to extend the simulation domain to include the conical contraction, the $90^\circ$ bend and a $\sim 1$~m extended straight pipe that replaces the RPT, and to test whether the design ranking established at the guide-vane trailing edge survives once the flow has passed through the bend;
  \item to analyse the unsteady rotor--stator interaction for the baseline configuration through a transient CFX simulation and a discrete Fourier transform of the force, torque and pressure monitor signals, and to identify the dominant spatial mode of the pulsation field at the blade-passing frequency.
\end{enumerate}
 
The corresponding research questions are stated in Chapter~\ref{chap:chap2} in the context of the literature, and the working hypotheses are formulated together with the results in Chapter~\ref{chap:chap5}.
 
\section{Scope and limitations}
\label{sec:chap1_scope}
 
Three deliberate boundaries define the scope. The simulation domain stops at the position where the RPT inlet would be; the RPT itself is the responsibility of the parallel master thesis by Dokset~\cite{Dokset2025} and a fully coupled RDT--GV--RPT screening over twenty-four configurations exceeds the available computational budget. All twenty-four configurations are simulated at a single nominal operating point ($\dot m = 290.3$~kg/s, $n = 650$~rpm), and only the baseline configuration is run as a transient simulation; the screening uses steady RANS with the SST $k$--$\omega$ turbulence model. The slip correction is held fixed at $\delta_{\mathrm{TE}} = 6^\circ$ for all configurations, in line with the recommendation by G\"ulich~\cite{Gulich2010}. Cavitation is treated only indirectly through the static-pressure field, and structural strength, fatigue and vibration of the guide vanes are not analysed beyond the unsteady-loading diagnostics in Chapter~\ref{chap:chap4}.

\section{Background and motivation}
\label{sec:background_motivation}
 
The European power system is undergoing the largest structural change in its history. Variable renewable generation, principally wind and solar power, is being deployed at a scale that is no longer compatible with the system-balancing methods of the past, and the European Green Deal explicitly tasks the union with both deep decarbonisation and a secure, affordable, and resilient supply~\cite{EC_EGD_2021}. The International Energy Agency reports that flexibility, not nameplate generation capacity, is becoming the binding constraint as renewable shares grow~\cite{IEA_Renewables2023}. This is a fundamentally different problem from the generation-adequacy concerns of the previous decades, and the technologies that solve it must be able to absorb energy when the wind and the sun deliver more than the load asks for, and release it again hours, days, or seasons later. Among the available options, only hydropower with reservoirs and pumped-storage hydropower (PSH) provide this service at a scale relevant to a continental power system at acceptable cost~\cite{IRENA_Storage2017}.
 
Hydropower remains the largest single source of renewable electricity worldwide. The 2025 World Hydropower Outlook reports approximately 4\,578~TWh of generation in 2024 from a global installed base of about 1\,443~GW, of which 189~GW is pumped storage after the addition of 8.4~GW in 2024 alone~\cite{IHA_WHO_2025}. The global pipeline of new pumped-storage projects is now close to 600~GW, and the annual rate of new commissioning has nearly doubled in two years. The trend confirms that pumped storage is no longer a niche balancing technology but is on its way to becoming a central pillar of the future low-carbon power system.
 
Norway holds a particular position in this landscape. Hydropower has dominated electricity supply in Norway since the early twentieth century and accounts for around 90\,\% of national production~\cite{RegjeringenVannkrafthistorie2019}. According to the Norwegian Water Resources and Energy Directorate (NVE), Norwegian reservoirs represent approximately half of all hydropower storage capacity in Europe, and a large share of national production is dispatchable due to extensive reservoir regulation~\cite{NVE_Fleksibilitet2023}. Norwegian hydropower is therefore a strategic balancing resource for both the domestic market and, through interconnection, the surrounding European system. This explains the strong research interest in upgrading existing Norwegian high-head hydropower plants for pumped-storage operation~\cite{DagsvikStorli2021}.
 
A cost-effective way of realising pumped storage in existing high-head schemes is to retrofit the powerhouse with a reversible pump-turbine (RPT) instead of the original Francis runner, while reusing the waterways, the surge chambers, and the reservoirs~\cite{DagsvikStorli2021}. The RPT operates as a Francis turbine when water flows from the upper to the lower reservoir and as a pump when rotation is reversed~\cite{Olimstad2012}. The dual-mode requirement is the central design tension of the machine: a runner that is well matched to turbine operation is rarely well matched to pump operation, and the cavitation limits in pump mode are stricter than in turbine mode. Dagsvik's work on RPT retrofits at NTNU shows that the available submergence in typical Norwegian high-head plants is often insufficient to give an adequate net positive suction head (NPSH) margin in pump mode, and that cavitation begins close to the desired operating range with the original civil structures unchanged~\cite{DagsvikThesis2024}.
 
Cavitation is therefore the dominant barrier to RPT retrofits at high-head sites~\cite{Franc2007}. The standard performance and cavitation tests prescribed by IEC~60193 require a clearly defined NPSH margin between the available head and the head at which a 1\,\% efficiency drop occurs~\cite{IEC60193_2019}. Recent measurements on the NTNU RPT confirm that the cavitation margin is the limiting design parameter~\cite{Kerlefsen2025}. Two engineering routes exist for relaxing this constraint. The first, lowering the runner physically by excavating the powerhouse, increases the static suction head but requires civil works that are typically prohibitive for an existing plant. The second is to install a booster pump in the draft tube upstream of the RPT, raising the local pressure in pump mode without altering the civil structure. Earlier work in the Store2Hydro project group has shown that a booster of moderate head ($H \approx 5$~m) is sufficient to bring the cavitation margin into a safe range while keeping the existing layout~\cite{Valstad2018, Hage2020, DagsvikThesis2024}.
 
A booster of this kind has unusual constraints. It must fit inside a circular draft tube with a diameter of about $0.5$~m, it must allow free passage of flow when not in use, and it should not introduce any element that prevents reverse rotation when the unit operates as a turbine. A conventional shaft-driven axial booster places obstacles in the centre of the duct and complicates the mechanical layout. The hubless rim-driven thruster (RDT) is a more recent class of axial machine that places the electric motor in the duct rim and drives the blades through a rotating ring, removing the central shaft entirely~\cite{Cao2012}. The hubless geometry leaves the centre of the duct open and therefore appears well suited to draft-tube installation. Within Store2Hydro, the hubless RDT has been adopted as the booster concept, and a sequence of project and master theses has developed the basic blade-element and two-dimensional optimisation methodologies for the RDT itself~\cite{Verdijck2025, Fjuk2025, Ambjornsson2025}.
 
The RDT, however, brings its own difficulty. As an axial-flow machine without a stator, the rotor leaves a strongly swirling and radially non-uniform wake at its discharge. Marine RDT studies show that the wake is dominated by helical tip and secondary vortices that persist many duct diameters downstream~\cite{Gaggero2023}, and that without a downstream stator a substantial part of the developed head is left in the flow as residual swirl rather than being recovered as useful pressure rise~\cite{Andersen2014}. Axial pre-rotation at the inlet of the RPT is known to affect both head and cavitation in pump mode: positive pre-rotation reduces the head and aggravates the cavitation margin, while negative pre-rotation may preserve head but is still associated with reduced efficiency relative to a swirl-free inflow~\cite{Jenssen2020, Larsen2019}. The natural design response is therefore to place a row of stationary swirl-recovery guide vanes (GVs) downstream of the RDT, between the booster and the RPT, with the dual purpose of reducing the residual swirl and converting tangential kinetic energy into static pressure before the flow reaches the RPT runner.
 
\section{Project work and the gap addressed by this thesis}
\label{sec:project_work_gap}
 
The present master thesis is a direct continuation of the project work \emph{Parametric Hydraulic Design of Guide Vanes for a Rim-Driven Thruster in Pumped-Storage Hydropower}~\cite{Leonsen2025}. The project work delivered three concrete contributions. It established a parametric MATLAB tool, \texttt{vanedesign\_loft\_export}, that generates NACA-type guide-vane sections, stacks them into three-dimensional vanes and exports them for CAD and CFD. It built a CFD-based slip database from idealised periodic-sector cascade simulations and condensed this into a smooth response-surface model that can be used as an optional trailing-edge correction. And it carried out a first comparative CFD study of two guide-vane geometries, denoted Case~A and Case~B, in a short straight duct downstream of the RDT, demonstrating the expected trade-off between swirl reduction and total-pressure loss.
 
This is a useful starting point but it falls short in three important respects. First, only two three-dimensional guide-vane geometries were assessed with CFD, and the project work explicitly lists the limited screening of chord, thickness, vane count and span as a methodological gap that should be closed in further work~\cite{Leonsen2025}. Two data points define a line through a trade-off; they do not define a Pareto front. Second, the duct domain used in the project work was a straight pipe without the geometric features that actually surround the guide vanes in the Store2Hydro layout, namely the conical contraction, the 90$^\circ$ bend, and the final inlet section before the RPT. The project work could therefore say something about the local performance of the guide vanes but very little about how their downstream effect develops through the rest of the suction-side waterway. Third, the parallel RPT pump-mode simulations carried out by Dokset~\cite{Dokset2025} used inlet profiles extracted \emph{downstream} of the guide vanes as their inlet condition, which means that the reported RPT performance numbers do not include the guide-vane passage drag itself~\cite{Leonsen2025}. The configuration that gives the most favourable RPT inflow is therefore not automatically the configuration that gives the most favourable full-system behaviour.
 
The present master thesis is constructed to close the first two of these gaps directly. The slip-correction methodology established in~\cite{Leonsen2025} is reused with a single fixed correction value, justified in Section~\ref{sec:rsi-thesis} and Chapter~\ref{chap:chap4}, in line with the recommendation by G\"ulich~\cite{Gulich2010} that a small built-in trailing-edge angle of about $4^\circ$--$6^\circ$ is sufficient to compensate for cascade deviation while avoiding the increased loading and separation risk associated with aggressive angle correction. The screening question is then attacked head-on by simulating a matrix of 24 guide-vane configurations spanning two design strategies (constant chord and constant solidity), four shroud-chord levels and two to three vane counts, all in the full RDT--GV--draft-tube domain that includes the $90^\circ$ bend and the final inlet pipe. The third gap, full-system performance with the RPT included, is left as future work, with the explicit justification that Dokset~\cite{Dokset2025} carries the parallel responsibility for the RPT side and that the available computational resources within the time frame of the present thesis do not allow a fully coupled RDT--GV--RPT campaign of the scale required for a screening study.
 
\section{Goal and research questions}
\label{sec:goal_research_q}
 
The main goal of this thesis is
 
\medskip
\begin{quote}
\textit{To develop a systematic CFD screening methodology for swirl-recovery guide vanes downstream of the rim-driven thruster in the Store2Hydro booster--RPT concept, and to use it to identify how the dominant design variables govern the trade-off between swirl reduction, hydraulic loss, and downstream flow quality through the conical section, the $90^\circ$ bend, and the final inlet section before the RPT.}
\end{quote}
\medskip
 
This goal is broken down into the following research questions:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item How do guide-vane design parameters --- vane count $Z_s$, chord-control strategy (constant chord versus constant solidity), and shroud-chord magnitude --- influence the swirl reduction, residual swirl distribution and total-pressure loss in the compact RDT--GV--draft-tube system?
  \item Which spanwise and plane-averaged metrics best discriminate between guide-vane designs that condition the inflow well for the downstream RPT and designs that do not?
  \item Is the design with the lowest residual tangential velocity directly downstream of the guide vanes also the design with the most favourable inflow at the end of the $90^\circ$ bend, or does the bend redistribute residual swirl in a way that changes the ranking?
  \item How robust is the design ranking when evaluated across several axial planes between the guide vanes and the RPT inlet, and what is the dominant mechanism that limits the achievable swirl reduction in this geometry?
\end{enumerate}
 
A working hypothesis is formulated for each question. It is hypothesised that increasing solidity, either by longer chord or by higher vane count, will reduce residual swirl monotonically but at a cost in total-pressure loss, and that the best designs will lie on a Pareto front rather than at the extreme of maximum swirl recovery. It is further hypothesised that residual swirl interacting with the secondary flow established in the $90^\circ$ bend will have a measurable effect on the ranking, and that designs with similar swirl numbers immediately downstream of the guide vanes can therefore differ markedly in inflow quality at the bend exit.
 
\section{Scope and limitations}
\label{sec:scope_limitations}
 
The scope is constrained both by what is most useful given previous work and by the available computational resources. Three explicit decisions define the boundary.
 
\paragraph{Decoupling from the RPT.} The simulation domain extends from the RDT inlet plane through the RDT, the guide-vane row, the conical contraction from $D = 0.5$~m to $D = 0.4$~m, the $90^\circ$ bend and a straight final inlet pipe of length $\sim 1.0$~m towards the RPT. The RPT itself is replaced by this final pipe, which acts as a buffer that prevents the imposed outlet boundary condition from contaminating the flow at the position where the RPT inlet would be. The RPT-side performance question is left to the parallel study by Dokset~\cite{Dokset2025} and is not within the scope of this thesis. This decision is justified in detail in Section~\ref{sec:rpt-decoupling-justification} and is consistent with the available computational budget; a fully coupled RDT--GV--RPT screening over 24 configurations was estimated to exceed the time-frame by a factor of approximately three.
 
\paragraph{Single operating point.} All 24 configurations are simulated at a single nominal operating point with mass-flow rate $\dot m = 290.3$~kg/s and rotor speed $n = 650$~rpm. Off-design behaviour is not investigated. The justification is that the goal of this work is comparative ranking of guide-vane geometries, not full operating maps, and that comparative ranking is meaningful even when carried out at a single representative point.
 
\paragraph{Steady RANS for the screening.} The 24-case screening uses steady RANS simulations with the SST $k$--$\omega$ turbulence model. A single transient simulation of the baseline configuration is included for the rotor--stator interaction analysis (Chapter~\ref{chap:chap4}), but a full transient screening campaign is not feasible within the time-frame.
 
\medskip
The same explicit limitations from the project work are inherited and are stated honestly here:
\begin{itemize}
  \item the RDT wake used to design the guide vanes corresponds to a single RDT geometry and a single operating point, taken from the simulation data sets \texttt{shubham\_blade6} and \texttt{shubham\_transient} as described in Chapter~\ref{chap:chap4};
  \item cavitation is not modelled in the screening campaign and is treated only indirectly through the static-pressure field and the cavitation considerations of Section~\ref{sec:theory_cavitation};
  \item structural strength, fatigue and vibration of the guide vanes are not analysed beyond the unsteady-loading diagnostics in Chapter~\ref{chap:chap4};
  \item the slip correction is fixed at the value $\delta_{\mathrm{TE}} = 6^\circ$ recommended by G\"ulich~\cite{Gulich2010} rather than swept across designs, in order to keep the screening matrix manageable.
\end{itemize}
 
These limitations mean that the present thesis should be read as a structured screening study that reduces a large design space to a small number of credible candidates and quantifies the dominant trade-off between swirl reduction and total-pressure loss in the realistic geometry of the Store2Hydro booster section. Selection of a final operating guide-vane geometry, a fully coupled RDT--GV--RPT simulation and a cavitation assessment of the chosen design are explicitly listed as future work in Chapter~\ref{chap:chap7}.
 
\section{Thesis outline}
\label{sec:thesis_outline}
 
The remainder of the thesis is organised as follows. Chapter~\ref{chap:chap2} reviews the literature on rim-driven thrusters, swirling pipe flow, guide-vane and axial diffuser design, slip and deviation correlations, swirl decay in pipes and the secondary flow produced by curved ducts. Chapter~\ref{chap:chap3} establishes the theoretical framework, including the governing equations, the cylindrical-coordinate description of the flow, the swirl number and alternative swirl metrics, the cascade theory for guide vanes, and the Dean-vortex theory of swirl development through a $90^\circ$ bend. Chapter~\ref{chap:chap4} describes the methodology, including the 24-case design matrix, the parametric MATLAB workflow, the multi-domain CFX setup with TurboGrid for the RDT and GV blade rows, the boundary conditions, the convergence criteria and the discretisation-uncertainty estimation procedure for the draft-tube domain. Chapter~\ref{chap:chap5} presents the simulation results and the comparative analysis of the 24 configurations, including the Pareto-front analysis between swirl reduction and head loss, the spanwise and plane-averaged diagnostics, and the unsteady rotor--stator analysis of the baseline configuration. Chapter~\ref{chap:chap6} concludes and Chapter~\ref{chap:chap7} states the recommended directions for future work.
