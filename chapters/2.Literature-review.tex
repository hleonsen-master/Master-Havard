\section{Booster-RPT systems: previous studies}
\label{sec:lit_booster_open}
 
The cavitation barrier on RPT retrofits has been recognised in the Norwegian hydropower research community~\cite{DagsvikThesis2024, Kerlefsen2025}. Previous studies support the use of a draft-tube booster pump as a possible way to improve the pump-mode cavitation margin. Valstad~\cite{Valstad2018} showed for the Roskrepp plant that a dedicated axial booster in series with the RPT can raise the pump-mode inlet pressure sufficiently to improve the cavitation margin. Follow-up studies on contra-rotating axial boosters by Hage~\cite{Hage2020} and Dahle~\cite{Dahle2019} confirmed that the necessary head increase is mechanically achievable. They also reported a more difficult finding: cavitation margin and global efficiency depend on \emph{how} the booster delivers the flow to the RPT, not only on how much head it adds. Strong residual swirl and radially non-uniform discharge profiles downstream of contra-rotating boosters were associated with low hydraulic efficiency~\cite{Dahle2019}. Similar observations appear in early Store2Hydro RDT studies~\cite{Fjuk2025, Ambjornsson2025}.
 
Two limitations recur across these studies. First, the booster--RPT pair is treated as two machines in series, with no element between them to condition the flow. The flow leaving the booster is taken as it is, and the RPT receives it. Second, where individual designers have considered discharge quality, the analysis has been qualitative. ``improved discharge quality'' is recommended without a quantitative target. The project work preceding this thesis addressed the first limitation by introducing a guide-vane row between the RDT and the RPT. The second limitation persisted: only two example geometries were simulated. Two design points define a line through a trade-off; they do not map it.
 
The present thesis addresses this limitation by screening a broad set of GV configurations across the relevant design space. The design matrix includes short and long chord lengths, constant-chord and constant-solidity strategies, and several vane counts. The purpose is not to fine-optimise a single best geometry. Instead, the aim is to identify the main hydraulic trends and trade-offs that govern swirl recovery, total-pressure loss and downstream flow quality. The screening campaign described in Chapter~\ref{chap:theory_method} simulates 18 geometries under identical operating and boundary conditions. The results are interpreted as a sampled recovery-loss trade-off, not as a final optimisation of one geometry.
 
\section{Stator design as a multi-objective optimisation problem}
\label{sec:lit_pareto}
 
Multi-objective optimisation has been the standard formulation for stator-vane and runner-blade design in compressors and gas turbines since the early 2000s. Conflicting cost functions (efficiency, loading, off-design margin, structural stress, noise) are traded against each other rather than collapsed into a weighted sum. Vasilopoulos and co-workers~\cite{Vasilopoulos2019} provide a representative formulation for turbomachinery shape optimisation. They trace the Pareto front in two-objective problems using a gradient-based algorithm coupled to an adjoint solver. The front is more useful than any single ``optimum'' because it makes the residual trade-off explicit. The hydraulic-machine community has adopted the same idea more recently. Pareto fronts are now a standard output of CFD-based Francis-runner and stay-vane optimisations~\cite{Schnoes2018}.
 
The most relevant precedent for the present configuration is in marine propulsion. Gaggero and Martinelli~\cite{GaggeroMartinelli2024} studied the nozzle and stator of a rim-driven pumpjet thruster using simulation-based design optimisation. They formulated the stator design as a multi-objective optimisation that minimises resistance while keeping the cavitation inception index low. A Pareto front was traced using a genetic algorithm coupled to a parametric stator description and a RANS solver. The geometry differs in detail from a hydropower booster, but the design \emph{problem} is the same. A rim-driven rotor leaves a swirling wake. A stator row recovers swirl. The trade-off is between recovery and loss. The screening must identify which member of a continuous family best balances the two.
 
Two implications follow. First, the screening output should be a Pareto-front map of swirl reduction against total-pressure loss, not a verdict on a single best geometry. Second, the trade-off shapes reported by~\cite{GaggeroMartinelli2024} for marine rim-driven stators can be compared qualitatively with the trade-off obtained here for the hydropower booster.
 
\section{The hubless rim-driven thruster wake as the design input}
\label{sec:lit_rdt_wake}
 
The hubless rim-driven thruster (RDT) was originally developed for maritime use, mostly as a thruster for navigating. Its key feature for the present application is the absence of a central hub. The hub-free centre keeps the duct centre open during reverse-flow operation in turbine mode. The hydrodynamic cost appears in the wake. Without a central hub, the rotor does not impose a solid inner-radius boundary on the flow. The inner region is therefore affected by vortical structures that migrate inwards from the free inner blade ends, together with a low-momentum or reverse-flow region near the centreline~\cite{Gaggero2023}.
 
The consequence for this thesis is that the inflow to the GV row is not a clean axisymmetric profile. It cannot be matched by a smooth metal-angle distribution alone. Leonsen~\cite{Leonsen2025} reports preliminary RDT wake data from Sharma and Djupesland, where the back-flow radius was estimated as \(r < 0.144~\mathrm{m}\). The longer transient RDT-only simulation used as design input in the present thesis refined this estimate and showed that the reverse axial flow was confined to the inner hub-pipe region, approximately \(r \lesssim 0.120~\mathrm{m}\). The inlet field still contains local absolute flow angles of about \(80^\circ\)--\(89^\circ\) near the outer radius. Any GV design that operates in this wake must therefore be treated as a hubless annular cascade, with the inner design radius placed outside the reverse-flow region.
 
Prior work on hubless cascades is limited. Andersen's hubless rim-driven waterjet pump~\cite{Andersen2014} is the closest precedent. It uses downstream stator vanes for swirl recovery, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:rdt_wake_and_vanes}. Zhai et al.~\cite{Zhai2022} further show that the duct contour influences the wake structure that a downstream stator must accommodate.

The wake is therefore not a free design variable. It is a fixed input drawn from one set of RDT simulations. The comparative ranking of the 18 GV configurations is performed against the same wake. A different RDT wake would likely shift the Pareto front and could also change the preferred vane geometry.
 
The hubless free-end mechanism deserves a separate note. The outer blade tip in a rim-driven thruster is attached to the rim, so the classical tip-leakage vortex is strongly reduced or absent. The open inner blade end can instead generate root or free-end vortical structures. These structures interact with the nozzle shear layer and the blade wakes~\cite{Gaggero2023}. Song et al.~\cite{Song2015} compared hub-type and hubless thrusters by CFD. The hubless rotor was the more efficient, but its suspended blade root shed a distinct vortex. This distinction is important for the GV inner cut-out radius and is revisited in Chapter~\ref{chap:theory_method}.
 
\section{Pre-rotation sensitivity of the RPT in pump mode}
\label{sec:lit_prerotation}
 
The residual swirl after the GVs is important because the downstream RPT is sensitive to pre-rotation. Larsen~\cite{Larsen2019} reports that pre-rotation did not increase efficiency in pump mode. Negative pre-rotation (NPR), opposite to the runner rotation, increased head. Positive pre-rotation (PPR), in the runner-rotation direction, reduced head. Jenssen~\cite{Jenssen2020} simulated the same NTNU RPT in pump mode with imposed forced-vortex pre-rotation profiles. At the high-flow operating point, NPR reduced the required cavitation number by up to 25.2\,\%. PPR increased it by up to 93\,\%. A small NPR is therefore less harmful than a small PPR of the same magnitude.
 
The sign-dependent response rules out a design objective based only on the magnitude of residual swirl. Zero swirl remains the safest baseline, and the comparative ranking in Chapter~\ref{chap:results_discussion} reports both magnitude and sign. A small NPR is treated as more favourable than a small PPR of the same magnitude. This also limits how much of the Francis-turbine GV tradition~\cite{Brekke2003, Brekke2001} can be transferred directly. Francis guide vanes are designed to impose a prescribed pre-rotation on a matched runner. Here the runner is fixed, and the vane row must condition the flow for that runner.
 
\section{Swirling flow through $90^\circ$ bends}
\label{sec:lit_bend}
 
The downstream geometry includes a $90^\circ$ bend between the GV section and the RPT inlet. This element is part of the design \emph{system}. Residual swirl from the vane row interacts with the bend before reaching the RPT. The pipe-flow community has covered this interaction extensively, but it has not entered the booster-RPT design discussion.
 
A bend cannot balance its own radial pressure gradient uniformly across the
section. The flow develops a pair of counter-rotating cells in the cross-stream
plane, the Dean vortices, whose strength scales with the Dean number
\[\mathrm{De} = \mathrm{Re}\sqrt{\frac{D_h}{2R_c}},\]
with $\mathrm{Re}$ the Reynolds number, $D_h$ the hydraulic diameter and $R_c$
the bend curvature radius~\cite{Dean1927}. Kim, Yadav and Kim~\cite{KimYadavKim2014}
simulated this secondary flow in a $90^\circ$ elbow in OpenFOAM and benchmarked
several turbulence models against experiment. Their finding is twofold: the
post-bend swirl intensity is a strong function of the curvature ratio and a weak
function of Reynolds number, and its downstream decay is close to exponential.
Dutta et al.~\cite{Dutta2025DeanVortex} captured the same vortex pair in a turbulent bend at curvature ratio $1.25$ with a $k$--$\varepsilon$ model.
 
When the upstream flow already carries swirl, the bend response is more complicated than for axial inflow. Kalpakli and \"Orl\"u~\cite{KalpakliOrlu2013} show experimentally that imposed swirl changes the secondary motion downstream of a \(90^\circ\) bend and can drive the two Dean cells towards a single-cell structure. The residual swirl entering the bend is therefore relevant for the downstream ranking, not only the swirl directly after the guide vanes.
 
Within the Store2Hydro project, Jenssen~\cite{Jenssen2020} observed bend-induced separation and asymmetric cross-stream profiles in the original RPT draft-tube geometry. Turbulence-model limitations in this regime are treated separately in Section~\ref{sec:lit_cfd}.
 
Two implications follow. First, the post-vane plane is not the only location at which the comparative ranking should be evaluated. Planes downstream of the bend must also be reported. Second, the sign of the residual swirl entering the bend is a relevant design variable. The discussion of the screening result in Chapter~\ref{chap:results_discussion} retains this distinction.
 
\section{Swirl decay in straight pipes}
\label{sec:lit_swirl_decay}

Between the GV trailing edge and the bend, the residual swirl evolves through a short duct section. Kitoh~\cite{Kitoh1991} showed that swirl intensity in a straight pipe decays slowly and approximately exponentially with axial distance (the relation is given in Section~\ref{sec:theory_swirl_decay}), so natural decay removes far less swirl than the GV row itself and the GV ranking based on swirl reduction remains meaningful.
 
\section{Cascade closure: solidity, diffusion limits and deviation}
\label{sec:lit_cascade}
 
The blade-element method underlying the parametric GV construction is classical~\cite{Wallis1968}. It is reused here from~\cite{Leonsen2025}. The closure relations cover solidity, diffusion-factor limits, and the deviation correction at the trailing edge. They are revisited in Chapter~\ref{chap:theory_method}.
 
Two classical loading criteria are standard screening tools for axial compressor and stator design. The diffusion-factor limit due to Lieblein, Schwenk and Broderick~\cite{Lieblein1953} sets $D_f \lesssim 0.6$. The de Haller bound on the relative-velocity ratio sets $W_2/W_1 \geq 0.72$. In the present application both are exceeded over part of the design span by all configurations on the Pareto front. The required turning is large near the inner radius. The two criteria are therefore retained as comparative indicators rather than as design constraints, consistent with practice in highly loaded axial machines~\cite{Schnoes2018, Leonsen2025}.
 
For the underlying hydrofoil profiles, the classical reference is Abbott and von Doenhoff~\cite{Abbott1959}. The NACA four-digit family and the modified NACA 00xx thickness law used in this thesis follow their construction directly. The recommended trailing-edge thickness ratio of $\sim 0.005c$ and the leading-edge roundness convention are inherited from the same source, in line with the practice in modern axial-pump design~\cite{Gulich2010}.
 
For deviation, Beaubert et al.~\cite{Beaubert2015, Beaubert2016} document the dependence of deviation on turning angle, solidity and Reynolds number for swirl-generating vanes in straight pipes. The project work~\cite{Leonsen2025} produced a CFD-based deviation database condensed into a response-surface model. The present thesis does not develop the deviation model further. It adopts G\"ulich's~\cite{Gulich2010} recommendation of a small fixed correction in the range $4^\circ$-$6^\circ$. The value $\delta_{\mathrm{TE}} = 6^\circ$ is used uniformly across all 18 configurations. At high turning angles, the response-surface prediction recommends aggressive correction. This increases the suction-side adverse pressure gradient and tends to promote separation. Such separation is undesirable in a screening campaign that aims to compare geometric effects without a confounding deviation variable.
 
\section{Numerical framework for swirling internal flows}
\label{sec:lit_cfd}
 
The numerical framework adopted for the screening is steady RANS in ANSYS CFX with the SST $k$-$\omega$ turbulence model of Menter~\cite{Menter1994}. SST $k$-$\omega$ is the industrial default for hydraulic turbomachinery. Strong swirling flow remains difficult for RANS regardless. Kitoh~\cite{Kitoh1991} showed that turbulent swirling pipe flow is highly anisotropic, and Escue and Cui~\cite{EscueCui2010} showed that two-equation eddy-viscosity models reproduce the mean flow only partially and tend to underpredict turbulence decay. The CFD results should therefore be interpreted as comparative design data, not as exact predictions of all secondary-flow structures.
 
The discretisation uncertainty for the unstructured draft-tube domain is estimated using the Grid Convergence Index (GCI). The procedure follows Celik et al.~\cite{Celik2008} and is based on the method of Roache~\cite{Roache1998}. The procedure is applied to the swirl number at the bend exit (Section~\ref{sec:gci_drafttube}). The structured turbomachinery meshes for the RDT and each GV row are generated with ANSYS TurboGrid. These meshes are assessed with mesh-quality metrics and near-wall resolution rather than a full triplet-based GCI for each design. A separate GCI study for all 18 blade-row meshes would be computationally expensive and was not considered cost-effective for the screening.
 
\section{Rotor-stator interaction and pressure pulsations}
\label{sec:lit_rsi}
 
The closely placed RDT-GV configuration places the rotor and the stator within an axial distance of the same order as the chord. This is the regime in which rotor-stator interaction (RSI) becomes a primary source of unsteady loading. Brekke's monograph~\cite{Brekke2010, Brekke2001} reviews the historical record of RSI-related vibration and pressure-pulsation problems in Francis machines. He also provides order-of-magnitude thresholds for acceptable pulsation amplitudes. The classical theoretical framework is the Tyler-Sofrin analysis~\cite{Tyler1962}, which decomposes the unsteady pressure field around a rotor-stator stage into spinning circumferential modes set by the rotor and stator blade counts. Its central design consequence is that equal counts, $Z_r = Z_s$, place an axisymmetric breathing mode at the blade-passing frequency that loads all stator vanes in phase, whereas coprime counts cancel the net axial force on the ring by circumferential symmetry. The modal decomposition and the mode-order formula are derived in Section~\ref{sec:rsi-tyler}.
 
The original Store2Hydro baseline used $Z_r = Z_s = 7$, which the Tyler-Sofrin argument flags as unfavourable on first principles. Whether the breathing mode actually dominates depends on how closely the RDT wake satisfies $Z_r$-fold azimuthal symmetry. Chapter~\ref{chap:results_discussion} tests this directly with two transient simulations (\texttt{CC\_450\_7} and \texttt{CC\_400\_6}), interpreted against the amplitude thresholds of Egusquiza et al.~\cite{Egusquiza2006} and the wet-natural-frequency considerations of Tanaka and Valentin~\cite{Tanaka2011, Valentin2015}.
 
\section{Identified gap and how this thesis addresses it}
\label{sec:lit_gap}


The reviewed literature converges on one gap. The booster--RPT system is mechanically feasible, but earlier studies place no conditioning element between the two machines, and the only CFD evidence for a guide-vane row covers two geometries, both in a straight-pipe domain, and comes from the project work preceding this thesis. The multi-objective optimisation tradition of the wider turbomachinery community~\cite{Vasilopoulos2019, Schnoes2018} has reached marine rim-driven stators~\cite{GaggeroMartinelli2024} but not the hydropower booster--RPT case. The present thesis brings a discrete screening to that case rather than a gradient-based search.

Three constraints set the design objective. The hubless RDT wake admits only a hubless cascade. The RPT responds to pre-rotation by its sign~\cite{Larsen2019, Jenssen2020}, so the target is conditioned residual swirl rather than its minimum. The downstream \(90^\circ\) bend interacts with residual swirl~\cite{KalpakliOrlu2013}, so the ranking must hold beyond the post-vane plane. The Tyler-Sofrin combinatorics~\cite{Tyler1962} mark the baseline $Z_r = Z_s = 7$ as a priori unfavourable.

The present thesis closes the gap with three elements. A structured screening of 18 GV configurations maps the recovery--loss trade-off. The CFD domain carries the flow through the $90^\circ$ bend and the extended draft-tube, so the ranking is checked downstream as well as at the vane. Two transient simulations test the Tyler-Sofrin prediction. The apparatus and methodology follow in Chapter~\ref{chap:theory_method}.
