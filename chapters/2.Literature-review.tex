\section{Booster--RPT systems: what previous studies leave open}
\label{sec:lit_booster_open}
 
The cavitation barrier on RPT retrofits has been recognised in the Norwegian hydropower research community for at least a decade~\cite{DagsvikThesis2024, Kerlefsen2025}. Previous studies support the use of a draft-tube booster pump as a possible way to improve the pump-mode cavitation margin. Valstad~\cite{Valstad2018} showed for the Roskrepp plant that a dedicated axial booster in series with the RPT can raise the pump-mode inlet pressure sufficiently to improve the cavitation margin. Follow-up studies on contra-rotating axial boosters by Hage~\cite{Hage2020} and Dahle~\cite{Dahle2019} confirmed that the necessary head increase is mechanically achievable. They also reported a less encouraging finding: cavitation margin and global efficiency are sensitive to \emph{how} the booster delivers the flow to the RPT, not only how much head it adds. Strong residual swirl and radially non-uniform discharge profiles downstream of contra-rotating boosters were associated with low hydraulic efficiency~\cite{Dahle2019}. Similar observations appear in early Store2Hydro RDT studies~\cite{Fjuk2025, Ambjornsson2025}.
 
Two limitations recur across these studies. First, the booster--RPT pair is treated as a system without an element placed in the gap to condition the flow. The flow leaving the booster is taken as it is, and the RPT receives it. Second, where individual designers have considered discharge quality, the analysis has been qualitative. ``improved discharge quality'' is recommended without a quantitative target. Earlier work by Leonsen~\cite{Leonsen2025} addressed the first limitation by introducing a guide-vane row between the RDT and the RPT. The second limitation persisted: only two example geometries were simulated. Two design points define a line through a trade-off; they do not map it.
 
The present thesis addresses this limitation by screening a broad set of GV configurations across the relevant design space. The design matrix includes short and long chord lengths, constant-chord and constant-solidity strategies, and several vane counts. The purpose is not to fine-optimise a single best geometry. Instead, the aim is to identify the main hydraulic trends and trade-offs that govern swirl recovery, total-pressure loss and downstream flow quality. The screening campaign described in Chapter~\ref{chap:theory_method} simulates 18 geometries under identical operating and boundary conditions. The results are interpreted as a sampled recovery--loss trade-off, not as a final optimisation of one geometry.
 
\section{Stator design as a multi-objective optimisation problem}
\label{sec:lit_pareto}
 
Compressor and gas-turbine designers have for at least two decades formulated stator-vane and runner-blade design as a multi-objective optimisation problem. Several conflicting cost functions --- efficiency, loading, off-design margin, structural stress, noise --- are traded against each other rather than collapsed into a single weighted sum. Vasilopoulos and co-workers~\cite{Vasilopoulos2019} provide a representative formulation for turbomachinery shape optimisation. They trace the Pareto front in two-objective problems using a gradient-based algorithm coupled to an adjoint solver. The front is more useful than any single ``optimum'' because it makes the residual trade-off explicit. The hydraulic-machine community has adopted the same idea more recently. Pareto fronts are now a standard output of CFD-based Francis-runner and stay-vane optimisations~\cite{Schnoes2018}.
 
The most relevant precedent for the present configuration is in marine propulsion. Lugaresi, Gaggero and Martinelli~\cite{LugaresiGaggeroMartinelli2024} studied the nozzle and stator of a rim-driven pumpjet thruster using simulation-based design optimisation. They formulated the stator design as a multi-objective optimisation that minimises resistance while keeping the cavitation inception index low. A Pareto front was traced using a genetic algorithm coupled to a parametric stator description and a RANS solver. The geometry differs in detail from a hydropower booster, but the design \emph{problem} is the same. A rim-driven rotor leaves a swirling wake. A stator row recovers swirl. The trade-off is between recovery and loss. The screening must identify which member of a continuous family best balances the two. Yang, Li and Zhang~\cite{Yang2024RDTAppendage} reach a similar conclusion. Their analysis of a downstream appendage on a marine RDT recommends that future RDT design assess such elements through structured parameter sweeps rather than individually motivated geometry changes.
 
Two implications follow. First, the screening output should be a Pareto-front map of swirl reduction against total-pressure loss, not a verdict on a single best geometry. Second, the marine RDT--stator literature provides a direct calibration. The trade-off shapes seen in~\cite{LugaresiGaggeroMartinelli2024, Yang2024RDTAppendage} can be compared qualitatively with the trade-off obtained here for the hydropower booster.
 
\section{The hubless rim-driven thruster wake as the design input}
\label{sec:lit_rdt_wake}
 
The hubless rim-driven thruster (RDT) was originally developed in the marine community~\cite{Cao2012, Song2015}. Its key feature for the present application is the absence of a central hub. The hub-free centre keeps the duct centre open during reverse-flow operation in turbine mode. The hydrodynamic price is paid in the wake. A hubless rotor cannot impose a defined inner-radius condition on the flow. The inner region is dominated by tip vortices migrating inwards along the inner free end of each blade, together with a low-momentum or back-flow zone along the centreline~\cite{Gaggero2023, Verdijck2025}.
 
The consequence for this thesis is that the inflow to the GV row is not a clean axisymmetric profile. It cannot be matched by a smooth metal-angle distribution. Leonsen~\cite{Leonsen2025} reports RDT wake data from Sharma and Djupesland. These data were used directly as the inlet condition for the screening. They show local absolute flow angles of about $80^\circ$--$89^\circ$ near the outer radius and a back-flow region for $r < 0.144$~m. Any GV design that operates in this wake must therefore be a hubless cascade. The inner radius must be cut off outside the back-flow region. Prior work on hubless cascades is limited. Andersen's hubless rim-driven waterjet pump~\cite{Andersen2014} is the closest precedent. It uses downstream stator vanes for swirl recovery, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:rdt_wake_and_vanes}. Zhai et al.~\cite{Zhai2022} further show that the duct contour influences the wake structure that a downstream stator must accommodate.

The wake is therefore not a free design variable. It is a fixed input drawn from one set of RDT simulations. The comparative ranking of the 18 GV configurations is performed against the same wake. A different RDT wake would likely shift the Pareto front and could also change the preferred vane geometry.
 
The hubless free-end mechanism deserves a separate note. In a rim-driven thruster, the outer blade tip is attached to the rim. The classical tip-leakage vortex is therefore strongly reduced or absent. The open inner blade end can instead generate root or free-end vortical structures. These structures interact with the nozzle shear layer and the blade wakes~\cite{Gaggero2023}. This distinction is important for the GV inner cut-out radius and is revisited in Chapter~\ref{chap:theory_method}.
 
\section{Pre-rotation sensitivity of the RPT in pump mode}
\label{sec:lit_prerotation}
 
The residual swirl after the GVs is important because the downstream RPT is sensitive to pre-rotation. Larsen~\cite{Larsen2019} reports that pre-rotation did not increase efficiency in pump mode. Negative pre-rotation (NPR), opposite to the runner rotation, increased head. Positive pre-rotation (PPR), in the runner-rotation direction, reduced head. Jenssen~\cite{Jenssen2020} simulated the same NTNU RPT in pump mode with imposed forced-vortex pre-rotation profiles. At the high-flow operating point, NPR reduced the required cavitation number by up to 25.2\,\%. PPR increased it by up to 93\,\%. A small NPR is therefore less harmful than a small PPR of the same magnitude.
 
This sign-dependent response means that the thesis cannot minimise only the magnitude of the residual swirl. Zero swirl remains the safest baseline. The comparative ranking in Chapter~\ref{chap:results_discussion} therefore reports both magnitude and sign. A small NPR is treated as more favourable than a small PPR of the same magnitude. This also limits how much of the Francis-turbine GV tradition~\cite{Brekke2003, Brekke2015} can be transferred directly. Francis guide vanes are designed to impose a prescribed pre-rotation on a matched runner. Here the runner is fixed, and the vane row must condition the flow for that runner.
 
\section{Swirling flow through $90^\circ$ bends}
\label{sec:lit_bend}
 
The downstream geometry includes a $90^\circ$ bend between the GV section and the RPT inlet. This element is part of the design \emph{system}. Residual swirl from the vane row interacts with the bend before reaching the RPT. The literature on this interaction is mature in the pipe-flow community but has not been brought into the booster--RPT design discussion.
 
In a plain bend the curvature imposes a radial pressure gradient that cannot be balanced uniformly. The flow develops a counter-rotating pair of secondary cells in the cross-stream plane: the Dean vortices, whose strength scales with the Dean number $\mathrm{De} = \mathrm{Re}\sqrt{D_h/2R_c}$~\cite{Dean1927}. Kim, Yadav and Kim~\cite{KimYadavKim2014} performed a benchmark CFD study of the secondary flow in a $90^\circ$ elbow. They showed that the post-bend swirl intensity depends strongly on the curvature ratio and only weakly on Reynolds number. The downstream dissipation is approximately exponential. Wang et al.~\cite{Wang2025DeanVortex} confirmed this picture for a turbulent bend at curvature ratio $1.25$.
 
When the upstream flow already carries swirl, the picture is more complicated. Liu and Vanierschot~\cite{LiuVanierschot2021} review the literature on swirling flow through curved pipes. They report that the Dean cells and the upstream swirl interact in a sign-dependent way. Depending on whether the swirl rotates with or against one of the Dean cells, that cell is amplified or suppressed. Escue and Cui~\cite{EscueCui2010} show that turbulence-model choice is important in swirling pipe flow. Their results indicate that eddy-viscosity models can reproduce parts of the mean flow at low swirl, while Reynolds-stress models become more suitable as swirl increases. Both model classes still show limitations in the decay of turbulence quantities. RANS models tend to underpredict turbulence anisotropy under strong rotation, which is a known limit on accuracy in this regime. Within the Store2Hydro project, Jenssen~\cite{Jenssen2020} observed bend-induced separation and asymmetric cross-stream profiles in the original RPT draft-tube geometry.
 
Two implications follow. First, the post-vane plane is not the only location at which the comparative ranking should be evaluated. Planes downstream of the bend must also be reported. Second, the sign of the residual swirl entering the bend is a relevant design variable. The discussion of the screening result in Chapter~\ref{chap:results_discussion} retains this distinction.
 
\section{Swirl decay in straight pipes}
\label{sec:lit_swirl_decay}
 
Between the GV trailing edge and the bend inlet, the residual swirl evolves through a straight pipe section. Kitoh~\cite{Kitoh1991} showed experimentally that the integrated swirl intensity in a straight circular pipe decays approximately exponentially with axial distance. The decay rate is not constant. It depends on the swirl intensity and Reynolds number. The implication for the present geometry is that the swirl immediately downstream of the GV row is not identical to the swirl at the bend inlet. Natural decay reduces the swirl number before the bend. This reduction is expected to be smaller than the reduction produced by the GV row itself. The GV ranking based on swirl reduction is therefore still meaningful, but it should also be checked at planes downstream of the bend.
 
\section{Cascade closure: solidity, diffusion limits and deviation}
\label{sec:lit_cascade}
 
The blade-element method underlying the parametric GV construction is classical~\cite{Wallis1968}. It is reused here from~\cite{Leonsen2025}. The closure relations --- solidity, diffusion-factor limits, and the deviation correction at the trailing edge --- are well established and are revisited in detail in Chapter~\ref{chap:theory_method}.
 
Two classical loading criteria are standard screening tools for axial compressor and stator design. The diffusion-factor limit due to Lieblein, Schwenk and Broderick~\cite{Lieblein1953} sets $D_f \lesssim 0.6$. The de Haller bound on the relative-velocity ratio sets $W_2/W_1 \geq 0.72$. In the present application both are exceeded over part of the design span by all configurations on the Pareto front. The required turning is large near the inner radius. The two criteria are therefore retained as comparative indicators rather than as design constraints, consistent with practice in highly loaded axial machines~\cite{Schnoes2018, Leonsen2025}.
 
For the underlying hydrofoil profiles, the classical reference is Abbott and von Doenhoff~\cite{Abbott1959}. The NACA four-digit family and the modified NACA 00xx thickness law used in this thesis follow their construction directly. The recommended trailing-edge thickness ratio of $\sim 0.005c$ and the leading-edge roundness convention are inherited from the same source, in line with the practice in modern axial-pump design~\cite{Gulich2010}.
 
For deviation, Beaubert et al.~\cite{Beaubert2015, Beaubert2016} document the dependence of deviation on turning angle, solidity and Reynolds number for swirl-generating vanes in straight pipes. The project work~\cite{Leonsen2025} produced a CFD-based deviation database condensed into a response-surface model. The present thesis does not develop the deviation model further. It adopts G\"ulich's~\cite{Gulich2010} recommendation of a small fixed correction in the range $4^\circ$--$6^\circ$. The value $\delta_{\mathrm{TE}} = 6^\circ$ is used uniformly across all 18 configurations. Aggressive correction following the response-surface prediction at high turning angles increases the suction-side adverse pressure gradient and tends to promote separation. Such separation is undesirable in a screening campaign that aims to compare geometric effects without a confounding deviation variable.
 
\section{Numerical framework for swirling internal flows}
\label{sec:lit_cfd}
 
The numerical framework adopted for the screening is steady RANS in ANSYS CFX with the SST $k$--$\omega$ turbulence model of Menter~\cite{Menter1994}. This is a common industrial choice for hydraulic turbomachinery. Strong swirling flow is still difficult for RANS models. Kitoh~\cite{Kitoh1991} showed that turbulent swirling pipe flow is highly anisotropic. Escue and Cui~\cite{EscueCui2010} showed that common turbulence models can reproduce parts of the mean flow, but can also give unrealistic turbulence decay. The CFD results should therefore be interpreted as comparative design data, not as exact predictions of all secondary-flow structures.
 
The discretisation uncertainty for the unstructured draft-tube domain is estimated using the Grid Convergence Index (GCI) procedure of Celik et al.~\cite{Celik2008}, based on the method of Roache~\cite{Roache1998}. The procedure is applied to the swirl number at the bend exit (Section~\ref{sec:gci_drafttube}). The structured turbomachinery meshes for the RDT and each GV row are generated with ANSYS TurboGrid. These meshes are assessed with mesh-quality metrics and near-wall resolution rather than a full triplet-based GCI for each design. A separate GCI study for all 18 blade-row meshes would be computationally expensive and was not considered cost-effective for the screening.
 
\section{Rotor--stator interaction and pressure pulsations}
\label{sec:lit_rsi}
 
The closely placed RDT--GV configuration places the rotor and the stator within an axial distance of the same order as the chord. This is the regime in which rotor--stator interaction (RSI) becomes a primary source of unsteady loading. Brekke's monograph~\cite{Brekke2010, Brekke2015} reviews the historical record of RSI-related vibration and pressure-pulsation problems in Francis machines. He also provides order-of-magnitude thresholds for acceptable pulsation amplitudes. The classical theoretical framework is the Tyler--Sofrin analysis~\cite{Tyler1962}, which decomposes the unsteady pressure field around a rotor--stator stage into circumferential modes of order $m_{k,j} = k Z_r - j Z_s$. The lowest mode at the blade-passing frequency ($k=1$) is $m_{\min} = \min_j |Z_r - j Z_s|$. Three regimes follow. When $\gcd(Z_r, Z_s) = 1$, the lowest mode is at least $|Z_r - Z_s|$ and the net axial force on the stator ring approaches zero by circumferential cancellation. When $Z_r = Z_s$, $m_{\min} = 0$ and the lowest mode is the axisymmetric breathing mode. All stator vanes are then loaded synchronously at $f_{\mathrm{BPF}}$.
 
The original Store2Hydro baseline used $Z_r = Z_s = 7$. On Tyler--Sofrin grounds this is an a priori unfavourable choice. Whether the breathing mode actually dominates the pulsation field in practice depends on how closely the RDT wake satisfies $Z_r$-fold azimuthal symmetry. The unsteady analysis in Chapter~\ref{chap:results_discussion} tests this question directly. Two transient simulations (\texttt{CC\_450\_7} and \texttt{CC\_400\_6}) are used. The analysis combines an FFT-based spectral decomposition of the force, torque and pressure monitor signals~\cite{Bergan2018}. A ring-coherence diagnostic distinguishes the $m=0$ breathing mode from spinning modes $|m| \geq 1$. Reference amplitude thresholds from Egusquiza et al.~\cite{Egusquiza2006} and the wet-natural-frequency considerations of Tanaka and Valentin~\cite{Tanaka2011, Valentin2015} are used to interpret the magnitudes obtained.
 
\section{Identified gap and how this thesis addresses it}
\label{sec:lit_gap}
 
The literature reviewed here supports the following synthesis. The booster--RPT system is technically attractive. Earlier studies treat the booster and the RPT as two machines without a conditioning element between them. A GV row is the natural response. The existing CFD evidence covers only two example geometries~\cite{Leonsen2025} and only in a straight-pipe domain. A multi-objective screening tradition exists in the wider turbomachinery community~\cite{Vasilopoulos2019}. It has recently been transferred to the marine rim-driven domain~\cite{LugaresiGaggeroMartinelli2024, Yang2024RDTAppendage}, but not to the hydropower booster--RPT case. The hubless RDT wake imposes a hubless cascade as the only feasible vane geometry. The downstream RPT is sensitive to pre-rotation in a sign-dependent way~\cite{Larsen2019, Jenssen2020}. The design objective is therefore conditioned residual swirl, not minimum residual swirl. The $90^\circ$ bend interacts non-trivially with residual swirl~\cite{LiuVanierschot2021}. The comparative ranking cannot be evaluated only at the post-vane plane. The RDT--GV configuration produces unsteady loading whose spatial-mode structure is set by the Tyler--Sofrin combinatorics~\cite{Tyler1962}. The original $Z_r = Z_s = 7$ choice is a priori unfavourable.
 
The master thesis closes this gap by combining three elements. The first is a structured screening over 18 GV configurations evaluated as a sampled recovery--loss trade-off. The second is a CFD domain that includes the $90^\circ$ bend and the extended draft-tube section. Residual swirl is reported both immediately downstream of the vanes and after the bend. The third is two transient simulations analysed under the Tyler--Sofrin framework. The theoretical apparatus and the methodology are developed in Chapter~\ref{chap:theory_method}.
