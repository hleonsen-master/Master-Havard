\section{Booster-RPT systems: previous studies}
\label{sec:lit_booster_open}
 
The cavitation barrier on RPT retrofits is well known in the Norwegian hydropower research community~\cite{DagsvikThesis2024, Kerlefsen2025}. A draft-tube booster pump is one proposed remedy. Valstad~\cite{Valstad2018} showed for the Roskrepp plant that a dedicated axial booster in series with the RPT can raise the pump-mode inlet pressure enough to improve the cavitation margin. Contra-rotating axial-booster studies by Hage~\cite{Hage2020} and Dahle~\cite{Dahle2019} confirmed that the necessary head increase is mechanically achievable. They also reported a more difficult finding: cavitation margin and global efficiency depend on \emph{how} the booster delivers the flow to the RPT, not only on how much head it adds. Dahle linked low hydraulic efficiency to strong residual swirl and radially non-uniform discharge profiles downstream of contra-rotating boosters~\cite{Dahle2019}. The early Store2Hydro RDT studies report the same pattern~\cite{Fjuk2025, Ambjornsson2025}.
 
Two limitations recur across these studies. First, the booster-RPT pair is treated as two machines in series, with no element between them to condition the flow. The flow leaving the booster is taken as it is, and the RPT receives it. Second, where individual designers have considered discharge quality, the analysis has been qualitative. ``Improved discharge quality'' is recommended without a quantitative target. The project work preceding this thesis addressed the first limitation by introducing a guide-vane row between the RDT and the RPT. The second limitation persisted: only two example geometries were simulated. Two design points define a line through a trade-off; they do not map it.
 
The present thesis closes that gap. The design matrix samples a broad set of GV configurations: short and long chord, constant-chord and constant-solidity strategies, and three vane counts. The aim is not to fine-optimise one geometry. It is to identify the main hydraulic trends and trade-offs that govern swirl recovery, total-pressure loss and downstream flow quality. The CFD campaign described in Chapter~\ref{chap:theory_method} simulates 18 geometries under identical operating and boundary conditions. The result is a sampled recovery-loss trade-off, not a single optimum.
 
\section{Stator design as a multi-objective optimisation problem}
\label{sec:lit_pareto}
 
Multi-objective optimisation has been the default formulation for stator-vane and runner-blade design in compressors and gas turbines for two decades. Conflicting cost functions (efficiency, loading, off-design margin, structural stress, noise) are traded explicitly against each other instead of collapsed into a weighted sum. Vasilopoulos and co-workers~\cite{Vasilopoulos2019} give a representative formulation. They trace the Pareto front in two-objective problems with a gradient-based algorithm coupled to an adjoint solver. The front is more useful than any single ``optimum'' because the residual trade-off stays visible. The hydraulic-machine community has adopted the same idea. Pareto fronts are now standard output in CFD-based Francis-runner and stay-vane optimisations~\cite{Schnoes2018}.
 
The most relevant precedent is in marine propulsion. Gaggero and Martinelli~\cite{GaggeroMartinelli2024} studied the nozzle and stator of a rim-driven pumpjet thruster using simulation-based design optimisation. They formulated the stator design as a multi-objective optimisation that minimises resistance while keeping the cavitation inception index low. The Pareto front was traced with a genetic algorithm coupled to a parametric stator description and a RANS solver. The geometry differs from a hydropower booster, but the design \emph{problem} is the same: a rim-driven rotor leaves a swirling wake that a downstream stator row must recover, with a trade-off between recovery and loss. The task is to identify which member of a continuous family best balances the two.
 
Two implications follow. The output should be a Pareto-front map of swirl reduction against total-pressure loss, not a verdict on a single best geometry. The trade-off shapes reported in~\cite{GaggeroMartinelli2024} for marine rim-driven stators offer a qualitative point of comparison for the hydropower booster results obtained here.
 
\section{The hubless rim-driven thruster wake as the design input}
\label{sec:lit_rdt_wake}
 
The hubless rim-driven thruster (RDT) was originally a maritime device, used mostly for navigation. The defining feature here is the absence of a central hub. An open centre lets the duct stay clear during reverse-flow operation in turbine mode. The hydrodynamic cost shows up in the wake. Without a central hub, the rotor does not impose a solid inner-radius boundary on the flow. The inner region is instead populated by vortical structures that migrate inwards from the free inner blade ends, together with a low-momentum or reverse-flow region near the centreline~\cite{Gaggero2023}.
 
The consequence for this thesis is that the inflow to the GV row is not a clean axisymmetric profile, and cannot be matched by a smooth metal-angle distribution alone. Leonsen~\cite{Leonsen2025} reports preliminary RDT wake data from Sharma and Djupesland, where the back-flow radius was estimated as \(r < 0.144~\mathrm{m}\). The longer transient RDT-only simulation used as design input in the present thesis covers more rotor revolutions and refines this estimate. It confines the reverse axial flow to the inner hub-pipe region, approximately \(r \lesssim 0.120~\mathrm{m}\). The inlet field still contains local absolute flow angles of \(80^\circ\)-\(89^\circ\) near the outer radius. Any GV design that operates in this wake is therefore a hubless annular cascade, with the inner design radius placed outside the reverse-flow region.
 
Prior work on hubless cascades is limited. Andersen's hubless rim-driven waterjet pump~\cite{Andersen2014} is the closest precedent. It uses downstream stator vanes for swirl recovery, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:rdt_wake_and_vanes}. Zhai et al.~\cite{Zhai2022} further show that the duct contour influences the wake structure that a downstream stator must accommodate.

The wake is therefore not a free design variable. It is a fixed input drawn from one set of RDT simulations, and the comparative ranking of the 18 GV configurations is performed against that same wake. A different RDT wake would likely shift the Pareto front and could change the preferred vane geometry.
 
One feature of the hubless geometry needs separate comment. The outer blade tip is attached to the rim, so the classical tip-leakage vortex is suppressed or absent. The open inner blade end can instead shed root or free-end vortical structures that interact with the nozzle shear layer and the blade wakes~\cite{Gaggero2023}. Song et al.~\cite{Song2015} compared hub-type and hubless thrusters by CFD: the hubless rotor was more efficient overall, but the suspended blade root shed a distinct vortex. This sets the inner cut-out radius for the GV and is revisited in Chapter~\ref{chap:theory_method}.
 
\section{Pre-rotation sensitivity of the RPT in pump mode}
\label{sec:lit_prerotation}
 
The residual swirl after the GVs matters because the downstream RPT is sensitive to pre-rotation. Larsen~\cite{Larsen2019} reports that pre-rotation did not improve efficiency in pump mode. Negative pre-rotation (NPR, opposite the runner direction) increased head; positive pre-rotation (PPR) reduced it. Jenssen~\cite{Jenssen2020} simulated the same NTNU RPT in pump mode with imposed forced-vortex pre-rotation profiles. At the high-flow operating point NPR cut the required cavitation number by up to 25.2\,\%, while PPR raised it by up to 93\,\%. A small NPR is therefore less harmful than a small PPR of the same magnitude.
 
The sign-dependent response rules out a design objective based on swirl magnitude alone. Zero swirl is still the safest baseline, and the ranking in Chapter~\ref{chap:results_discussion} reports both magnitude and sign. A small NPR is preferred over the matching PPR. This also limits how much of the Francis-turbine GV tradition can transfer~\cite{Brekke2003, Brekke2001}. Francis guide vanes impose a prescribed pre-rotation on a matched runner. Here, the runner is fixed; the vane row must condition the flow to suit it.
 
\section{Swirling flow through $90^\circ$ bends}
\label{sec:lit_bend}
 
The downstream geometry includes a $90^\circ$ bend between the GV section and the RPT inlet. This element is part of the design \emph{system}. Residual swirl from the vane row interacts with the bend before reaching the RPT. The pipe-flow community has covered this interaction extensively, but it has not entered the booster-RPT design discussion.
 
A bend cannot balance its own radial pressure gradient uniformly across the section. The flow develops a pair of counter-rotating cells in the cross-stream plane, the Dean vortices, whose strength scales with the Dean number
\[\mathrm{De} = \mathrm{Re}\sqrt{\frac{D_h}{2R_c}},\]
with $\mathrm{Re}$ the Reynolds number, $D_h$ the hydraulic diameter and $R_c$ the curvature radius~\cite{Dean1927}. Kim, Yadav and Kim~\cite{KimYadavKim2014} simulated this secondary flow in a $90^\circ$ elbow in OpenFOAM and benchmarked several turbulence models against experiment. They report that the post-bend swirl intensity scales strongly with the curvature ratio, only weakly with Reynolds number, and decays close to exponentially downstream. Dutta et al.~\cite{Dutta2025DeanVortex} captured the same vortex pair in a turbulent bend at curvature ratio 1.25 with a $k$-$\varepsilon$ model.
 
When the upstream flow already carries swirl, the bend response is more complicated than for axial inflow. Kalpakli and \"Orl\"u~\cite{KalpakliOrlu2013} show experimentally that imposed swirl changes the secondary motion downstream of a \(90^\circ\) bend and can drive the two Dean cells towards a single-cell structure. The residual swirl entering the bend is therefore relevant for the downstream ranking, not only the swirl directly after the guide vanes.
 
Within the Store2Hydro project, Jenssen~\cite{Jenssen2020} observed bend-induced separation and asymmetric cross-stream profiles in the original RPT draft-tube geometry. Turbulence-model limitations in this regime are treated separately in Section~\ref{sec:lit_cfd}.
 
Two implications follow. The post-vane plane alone is not enough for the comparative ranking; planes downstream of the bend must also be reported. And because the bend response depends on the sign of the residual swirl entering it, that sign becomes a relevant design variable, not just its magnitude. The discussion in Chapter~\ref{chap:results_discussion} keeps both distinctions.
 
\section{Swirl decay in straight pipes}
\label{sec:lit_swirl_decay}

Between the GV trailing edge and the bend, the residual swirl evolves through a short duct section. Kitoh~\cite{Kitoh1991} showed that swirl intensity in a straight pipe decays slowly and approximately exponentially with axial distance (the relation is given in Section~\ref{sec:theory_swirl_decay}), so natural decay removes far less swirl than the GV row itself, and the GV ranking based on swirl reduction stays meaningful.
 
\section{Cascade closure: solidity, diffusion limits and deviation}
\label{sec:lit_cascade}
 
The blade-element method underlying the parametric GV construction is classical~\cite{Wallis1968}. It is reused here from~\cite{Leonsen2025}. The closure relations cover solidity, diffusion-factor limits, and the deviation correction at the trailing edge. They are revisited in Chapter~\ref{chap:theory_method}.
 
Two classical loading criteria serve as standard tools for axial compressor and stator design. The Lieblein-Schwenk-Broderick diffusion-factor limit sets $D_f \lesssim 0.6$~\cite{Lieblein1953}, while de Haller's bound on the relative-velocity ratio requires $W_2/W_1 \geq 0.72$. Both are expected to be exceeded over part of the design span in the present application, since the required turning near the inner radius is large; Chapter~\ref{chap:results_discussion} confirms this for the configurations on the Pareto front. The two criteria are therefore retained as comparative loading indicators rather than design constraints, in line with practice for highly loaded axial machines~\cite{Schnoes2018, Leonsen2025}.
 
For the underlying hydrofoil profiles, the classical reference is Abbott and von Doenhoff~\cite{Abbott1959}. The NACA four-digit family and the modified NACA 00xx thickness law used in this thesis follow their construction directly. The recommended trailing-edge thickness ratio of $\sim 0.005c$ and the leading-edge roundness convention are inherited from the same source, in line with modern axial-pump practice~\cite{Gulich2010}.
 
For deviation, Beaubert et al.~\cite{Beaubert2015, Beaubert2016} document how deviation depends on turning angle, solidity and Reynolds number for swirl-generating vanes in straight pipes. The project work~\cite{Leonsen2025} produced a CFD-based deviation database and condensed it into a response surface. The present thesis does not develop that model further. It adopts G\"ulich's~\cite{Gulich2010} recommended fixed correction of $4^\circ$-$6^\circ$, applied uniformly as $\delta_{\mathrm{TE}} = 6^\circ$ across all 18 configurations. The response-surface prediction would call for a more aggressive correction at the high turning angles required here. That sharpens the suction-side adverse pressure gradient and tends to promote separation, which would confound the comparison of pure geometric effects across the matrix.
 
\section{Numerical framework for swirling internal flows}
\label{sec:lit_cfd}
 
The numerical framework is steady RANS in ANSYS CFX with Menter's SST $k$-$\omega$ model~\cite{Menter1994}, the industrial default for hydraulic turbomachinery. Strong swirling flow remains challenging for RANS regardless of the closure. Kitoh~\cite{Kitoh1991} demonstrated that turbulent swirling pipe flow is highly anisotropic, and Escue and Cui~\cite{EscueCui2010} show that two-equation eddy-viscosity models reproduce the mean flow only partially and tend to underpredict turbulence decay. The CFD results are therefore interpreted as comparative design data, not as exact predictions of every secondary-flow structure.
 
Discretisation uncertainty on the unstructured draft-tube domain is estimated via the Grid Convergence Index (GCI), following Celik et al.~\cite{Celik2008} after Roache~\cite{Roache1998}. The procedure is applied to the swirl number at the bend exit (Section~\ref{sec:gci_drafttube}). For the structured turbomachinery meshes, both the RDT and each GV row are generated in ANSYS TurboGrid. These meshes are assessed through mesh-quality metrics and near-wall resolution rather than a full triplet-based GCI per design, since running 18 separate GCI triplets on the blade rows is not cost-effective for a comparative campaign.
 
\section{Rotor-stator interaction and pressure pulsations}
\label{sec:lit_rsi}
 
The closely placed RDT-GV configuration sets the rotor and the stator within an axial distance of the same order as the chord. This is the regime in which rotor-stator interaction (RSI) becomes a primary source of unsteady loading. Brekke's monograph~\cite{Brekke2010, Brekke2001} reviews the historical record of RSI-related vibration and pressure-pulsation problems in Francis machines, and gives order-of-magnitude thresholds for acceptable pulsation amplitudes. The classical theoretical framework is the Tyler-Sofrin analysis~\cite{Tyler1962}, which decomposes the unsteady pressure field around a rotor-stator stage into spinning circumferential modes set by the rotor and stator blade counts. Its central design consequence is that equal counts, $Z_r = Z_s$, place an axisymmetric breathing mode at the blade-passing frequency that loads all stator vanes in phase, whereas coprime counts cancel the net axial force on the ring by circumferential symmetry. The modal decomposition and the mode-order formula are derived in Section~\ref{sec:rsi-tyler}.
 
The original Store2Hydro baseline used $Z_r = Z_s = 7$, which the Tyler-Sofrin argument flags as unfavourable on first principles. Whether the breathing mode actually dominates depends on how closely the RDT wake satisfies $Z_r$-fold azimuthal symmetry. Chapter~\ref{chap:results_discussion} tests this directly with two transient simulations (\texttt{CC\_450\_7} and \texttt{CC\_400\_6}), interpreted against the amplitude thresholds of Egusquiza et al.~\cite{Egusquiza2006} and the wet-natural-frequency considerations of Tanaka and Valentin~\cite{Tanaka2011, Valentin2015}.
 
\section{Identified gap and how this thesis addresses it}
\label{sec:lit_gap}

The reviewed literature converges on one gap. The booster-RPT system is mechanically feasible, but earlier studies place no conditioning element between the two machines, and to the author's knowledge the only CFD evidence for a guide-vane row covers two geometries, both in a straight-pipe domain, and comes from the project work preceding this thesis. The multi-objective optimisation tradition of the wider turbomachinery community~\cite{Vasilopoulos2019, Schnoes2018} has reached marine rim-driven stators~\cite{GaggeroMartinelli2024} but not the hydropower booster-RPT case. The present thesis brings a discrete screening to that case rather than a gradient-based search.

Three constraints set the design objective. The hubless RDT wake forces the cascade to be hubless too. The RPT response to pre-rotation depends on its sign~\cite{Larsen2019, Jenssen2020}, so the target is \emph{conditioned} residual swirl, not minimum swirl. Residual swirl also interacts with the downstream $90^\circ$ bend~\cite{KalpakliOrlu2013}; the ranking must therefore hold beyond the post-vane plane. And the Tyler-Sofrin combinatorics~\cite{Tyler1962} flag the baseline $Z_r = Z_s = 7$ as \emph{a priori} unfavourable.

The present thesis closes the gap with three elements. First, a structured CFD comparison of 18 GV configurations maps the recovery-loss trade-off. Second, the computational domain carries the flow through the $90^\circ$ bend and the extended draft tube, so the ranking can be checked downstream as well as at the vane. Third, two transient simulations test the Tyler-Sofrin prediction directly. The apparatus and methodology follow in Chapter~\ref{chap:theory_method}.
